Day 4 · Alternate Problem Set v2 — Songbirds

Ten problems on likelihood and Bayes — avian / songbird ecology

Novel problems covering MLE-by-hand derivations, Bayesian fits in Nimble, and conceptual questions on priors, identifiability, and credible intervals — all set in songbird and passerine ecology. Solutions sketched at the end.

Aim for ~3 hours total. Try each problem before checking the solution sketch. The first three are MLE-by-hand derivations; problems 4–7 are Bayesian models you fit in Nimble; problems 8–10 are conceptual.

Problem 1 · MLE by hand · Binomial · 25 min

Yellow Warbler nest survival

You monitored 24 Yellow Warbler nests through the egg-laying and incubation periods. Each nest either successfully hatched at least one chick ($y_i = 1$) or failed ($y_i = 0$). Out of 24 nests, 17 succeeded; the remaining 7 failed (depredation or abandonment).

  1. Write the joint likelihood as $L(\phi)$ where $\phi$ is the probability a nest succeeds. Show all steps.
  2. Differentiate the log-likelihood and solve for the MLE $\hat\phi$.
  3. Compute the observed Fisher information $I(\hat\phi)$ and Wald 95% CI.
  4. Compute the 95% profile likelihood CI in R using mle() and confint(). Compare to the Wald CI.
Nest-success probabilities in songbirds are often estimated this crudely as a first pass. Day 5 builds the proper "Mayfield" daily-survival framework on top of the same likelihood structure.
Problem 2 · MLE by hand · Poisson · 25 min

Black-capped Chickadee count MLE

During point-count surveys at $n = 30$ stations you observed counts $y_i$ of Black-capped Chickadees, totaling $\sum y_i = 84$ birds. Treat counts as iid Poisson($\lambda$).

  1. Write the joint likelihood. Take the log.
  2. Show the MLE is $\hat\lambda = \bar y = 84/30 = 2.8$.
  3. Show that the observed Fisher information at $\hat\lambda$ is $n/\hat\lambda$. Wald SE?
  4. An over-dispersion test: empirical variance of the $y_i$ is 7.5. Compute the dispersion ratio $\hat\phi = \widehat{\text{Var}(Y)}/\hat\lambda$ and comment on what that says about Poisson adequacy.
Problem 3 · MLE by hand + numerical · Mixture · 40 min

Hummingbird arrival times — two-component Normal mixture

You record the arrival time of $n=200$ Rufous Hummingbirds at a feeder over a single morning, measured in minutes after sunrise. Field intuition suggests two cohorts — early risers and a later wave — modeled as a 2-component Normal mixture:

$f(y \mid \pi, \mu_1, \mu_2, \sigma) = \pi\, \mathcal{N}(y; \mu_1, \sigma^2) + (1-\pi)\, \mathcal{N}(y; \mu_2, \sigma^2).$
  1. Why is the MLE for a mixture not a sum of two single-component MLEs? Be specific about which step in the derivation breaks down.
  2. Simulate one dataset under $(\pi, \mu_1, \mu_2, \sigma) = (0.4, 30, 90, 15)$ and fit the model with mle(). Constrain $\mu_1 < \mu_2$ to avoid label switching.
  3. Run mle() from 10 different starting values. How many local optima do you find? Report the global best and the fraction of fits that converged to it.
Problem 4 · Bayesian · Nimble · 30 min

Bayesian Hermit Thrush survival

You marked 50 adult Hermit Thrushes in spring; 38 returned the following year alive. Literature suggests adult passerine survival is around 0.55 with moderate uncertainty.

  1. Justify a Beta(11, 9) prior on $\phi$ (annual survival). What does its prior mean and effective sample size represent?
  2. Write the model in Nimble and fit it with nimbleMCMC() using 3 chains.
  3. Report posterior mean, median, 95% BCI, 95% HPDI for $\phi$.
  4. Compute $\Pr(\phi > 0.7 \mid \text{data})$. Compare to the closed-form Beta(49, 21) posterior to verify Nimble is doing what you think.
Problem 5 · Bayesian · Nimble · 30 min

Bayesian Poisson detection rate for Cerulean Warbler

Cerulean Warbler is a species of conservation concern. You ran 25 ten-minute point counts in mature mixed-mesophytic forest and recorded these per-count detection numbers:

y = c(0,1,0,2,0,1,0,0,3,1,0,0,1,2,0,1,0,1,0,0,2,0,1,1,0)

  1. Place a weakly informative $\text{Gamma}(\alpha=1, \beta=1)$ prior on $\lambda$, the mean detections per count.
  2. Fit the model in Nimble. Report the posterior mean and 95% BCI for $\lambda$.
  3. Compute $\Pr(\lambda < 1 \mid y)$. This is a statement about how rare detections are.
  4. Verify your Nimble fit against the closed-form Gamma$(1 + \sum y_i,\, 1 + n)$ posterior.
Problem 6 · Bayesian regression · Nimble · 45 min

Bayesian regression: White-crowned Sparrow mass on wing length

You captured 80 White-crowned Sparrows and recorded wing chord (mm) and mass (g). Simulate plausible data and fit a Bayesian Normal regression.

  1. Simulate $n=80$ observations with wing $\in U(60, 80)$, intercept $\beta_0 = -10$, slope $\beta_1 = 0.6$, and $\sigma = 2$ grams.
  2. Fit a Nimble model with $\beta_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 100)$, $\beta_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10)$, $\sigma \sim$ half-Normal(0, 10).
  3. Report posterior summaries. Are the truth values inside the 95% BCI?
  4. Add a posterior predictive dataset to your model (mass_rep[i] ~ dnorm(...)) and compute a Bayesian p-value using the standard deviation of residuals as the test statistic.
Problem 7 · Bayesian · Nimble · 40 min

House Wren occupancy — a single-season model

You visited 60 sites three times each in a single breeding season; you scored detection (1) or non-detection (0) of House Wrens at each visit. Let $\psi$ = occupancy probability and $p$ = per-visit detection probability conditional on occupancy.

  1. Write down the likelihood for site $i$: $\Pr(y_{i,1}, y_{i,2}, y_{i,3} \mid \psi, p)$. Hint: condition on occupancy state.
  2. Implement the model in Nimble with priors $\psi \sim$ Beta(1,1) and $p \sim$ Beta(1,1).
  3. Simulate a dataset with true $\psi = 0.55$ and $p = 0.4$. Fit your model. Are the truth values in the 95% BCI?
  4. What identifiability problem arises if every site is occupied and you never detect at any site? Sketch how the posterior behaves in that pathological case.
Occupancy models are the workhorse of avian point-count analysis. This is a preview of Day 5; the goal here is to see that the Bayesian implementation is just a few lines once you have the likelihood.
Problem 8 · Conceptual · prior sensitivity · 20 min

Prior sensitivity for Northern Spotted Owl detection

You observed 3 detections in 20 surveys. Compare four priors on $p$: Beta(1,1) (flat), Beta(1,9) (skeptical, prior mean 0.10), Beta(20, 30) (informative around 0.40), and Beta(100, 100) (very informative around 0.50).

  1. For each prior, write down the posterior in closed form.
  2. Plot all four posteriors on the same axes; comment on which is closest to the data MLE and which is most shifted.
  3. Suppose you read a paper that fit this same Spotted Owl dataset and reported a posterior mean of 0.49. Which prior most likely produced that result, and what concerns would you raise as a reviewer?
Problem 9 · Conceptual · BCI vs HPDI · 15 min

BCI vs HPDI for a skewed posterior on clutch size

You fit a Poisson model to clutch sizes of Chestnut-sided Warbler nests, $n = 12$ nests, $\sum y = 38$, prior Gamma(2,1). The posterior on the mean clutch size $\lambda$ is Gamma($2 + 38, 1 + 12$) = Gamma(40, 13).

  1. Compute the posterior mean, mode, median, 95% BCI, and 95% HPDI for $\lambda$.
  2. Plot the posterior and overlay both intervals.
  3. Why are BCI and HPDI almost identical here, but would be substantially different if the prior had been Gamma(1, 1) with very small data?
Problem 10 · Conceptual · identifiability · 25 min

Identifiability and label switching in mixture models

You fit the Hummingbird arrival-time mixture from Problem 3 in Nimble instead of with mle(). With 3 chains and no constraint on the order of $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$, your trace plots for the parameter "$\mu_1$" jump between two values across iterations and between chains.

  1. Explain what is happening, geometrically. Why would Rhat be enormous even though the algorithm has "converged"?
  2. Propose three ways to fix the problem. For each, describe one limitation.
  3. If you reorder posterior draws so that $\mu_1 < \mu_2$ always (a post hoc relabeling), what bias might you introduce? When is post hoc relabeling justified?

Solution sketches

Problem 1 sketch

(a) $L(\phi) = \phi^{17}(1-\phi)^7$.

(b) $\hat\phi = 17/24 \approx 0.708$. (c) $I(\hat\phi) = 24/[\hat\phi(1-\hat\phi)] \approx 116.4$; SE $\approx 0.093$; Wald 95% CI $\approx (0.526, 0.890)$.

(d) mle() + confint() gives profile CI ≈ (0.503, 0.864) — narrower at the bottom because the curve is steeper there.

Problem 2 sketch

(a) $L(\lambda) = \prod \dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^{y_i}}{y_i!} = e^{-n\lambda}\lambda^{\sum y_i}/\prod y_i!$. $\ell(\lambda) = -n\lambda + (\sum y_i)\log\lambda + C$.

(b) $\ell'(\lambda) = -n + \sum y_i / \lambda = 0 \Rightarrow \hat\lambda = \bar y = 2.8$.

(c) $\ell''(\lambda) = -\sum y_i / \lambda^2$, $-\ell''(\hat\lambda) = n / \hat\lambda$. SE = $\sqrt{\hat\lambda/n} = \sqrt{2.8/30} \approx 0.306$.

(d) $\hat\phi = 7.5/2.8 \approx 2.7$. Variance is ~2.7× the mean — substantial over-dispersion. Poisson SE is too small; switch to NB or include a random effect.

Problem 3 sketch

(a) The single-component MLE relies on $\log\prod f(y_i; \theta) = \sum \log f(y_i; \theta)$. With a mixture, $\log[\pi f_1 + (1-\pi)f_2]$ is not separable in the parameters — the log doesn't distribute over the sum. The MLE has no closed form.

(b) Simulate and fit (with $\mu_1 < \mu_2$). Typical: estimates near (0.40, 30, 90, 15) when starts are reasonable.

(c) From varied starts, you may find 2 local optima; the global is usually visited by ≥ 70% of starts. Practical rule: run 10–50 starts and keep the lowest NLL.

Problem 4 sketch

(a) Beta(11, 9): prior mean $11/20 = 0.55$, prior pseudo-sample size 20 (modest). Implies "I've seen the equivalent of 20 prior trials with 11 survivors."

(b–c) Posterior Beta(49, 21). Mean ≈ 0.700, BCI ≈ (0.59, 0.79), HPDI nearly identical.

(d) $\Pr(\phi > 0.7) = 1 - \text{pbeta}(0.7, 49, 21) \approx 0.49$.

Problem 6 sketch
library(nimble); library(MCMCvis)
set.seed(5); n <- 80
wing <- runif(n, 60, 80)
mass <- -10 + 0.6 * wing + rnorm(n, 0, 2)

reg <- nimbleCode({
 beta0 ~ dnorm(0, sd = 100)
 beta1 ~ dnorm(0, sd = 10)
 sigma ~ T(dnorm(0, sd = 10), 0, )
 for (i in 1:n) {
 mu[i] <- beta0 + beta1 * wing[i]
 mass[i] ~ dnorm(mu[i], sd = sigma)
 mass_rep[i] ~ dnorm(mu[i], sd = sigma)
 }
 obs_sd <- sd(mass[1:n])
 rep_sd <- sd(mass_rep[1:n])
})
out <- nimbleMCMC(reg, constants = list(n = n, wing = wing),
 data = list(mass = mass),
 inits = list(beta0 = 0, beta1 = 0, sigma = 1),
 monitors = c("beta0","beta1","sigma","obs_sd","rep_sd"),
 nchains = 3, nburnin = 3000, niter = 12000,
 samplesAsCodaMCMC = TRUE)
MCMCsummary(out)
mean(MCMCchains(out, "rep_sd") >= MCMCchains(out, "obs_sd")) # Bayesian p, ~0.5
Problem 7 sketch

(a) If $Z_i$ is the occupancy state: $\Pr(y_i \mid \psi, p) = \psi \prod_t p^{y_{it}}(1-p)^{1-y_{it}} + (1-\psi) \mathbf{1}\{\sum_t y_{it} = 0\}$.

occ <- nimbleCode({
 psi ~ dbeta(1,1); p ~ dbeta(1,1)
 for (i in 1:N) {
 Z[i] ~ dbern(psi)
 for (t in 1:T) y[i,t] ~ dbern(Z[i] * p)
 }
})

(d) If $\psi = 1$ but $p$ is very small you may see all zeros — the data cannot distinguish "absent" from "present-but-undetected". The posterior on $\psi$ stays near the prior; intervals are wide.

Problem 8 sketch

Data: $y = 3$, $n = 20$, MLE $= 0.15$. Posteriors:

  • Beta(1,1) → Beta(4, 18), mean ≈ 0.182.
  • Beta(1,9) → Beta(4, 26), mean ≈ 0.133.
  • Beta(20,30) → Beta(23, 47), mean ≈ 0.329.
  • Beta(100,100) → Beta(103, 117), mean ≈ 0.468.

A reported posterior mean of 0.49 would most plausibly come from a strong prior like Beta(100,100). As a reviewer, you'd ask why such a strong prior was used and demand a sensitivity analysis.

Problem 9 sketch

Gamma(40, 13): mean ≈ 3.08, mode = 39/13 ≈ 3.00, median ≈ 3.05. BCI ≈ (2.20, 4.08), HPDI ≈ (2.18, 4.04). Nearly identical because the Gamma is only modestly skewed when shape is large.

With Gamma(1, 1) + 1 observation $y = 0$, posterior is Gamma(1, 2), exponential-shaped. BCI = (0.013, 1.84); HPDI = (0, 1.50) — the HPDI is anchored at zero by the density's peak, the BCI splits tails symmetrically.

Problem 10 sketch

(a) The two components are exchangeable: any draw with $(\mu_1, \mu_2) = (30, 90)$ has identical posterior density to $(90, 30)$. Chains hop between modes, $\hat R$ explodes because chain-means differ. The model fit is fine; the parameter labels are not.

(b) Fixes (each with limitation): (i) constrain $\mu_1 < \mu_2$ in the model via truncation — limits prior flexibility; (ii) initialize all chains in the same labeled mode and use a sampler that can't tunnel — only works for well-separated components; (iii) post hoc relabel each draw so $\mu_1 < \mu_2$ — may distort posteriors of $\pi$ when components are close together.

(c) Post hoc relabeling assumes the underlying components are distinguishable. If components overlap heavily, relabeling biases $\pi$ toward 0.5 and shrinks the $\mu$'s toward each other. Justified when components are visibly well-separated.
