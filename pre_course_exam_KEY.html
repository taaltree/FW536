Pre-course exam — answer key

Worked solutions, point values, scoring rubrics, and instructor notes for the FW 536 Day-1-morning pre-course exam. Pair this with post_course_exam_KEY.html when computing pre/post gain.

Scoring guidance

The pre-course exam is intentionally scored generously. Use the following heuristic:

  • Full credit (= listed pts): correct answer, plausible reasoning.
  • Partial credit (≈ half pts): correct setup but arithmetic / interpretation error; or close-but-wrong intuition that shows the student grasped the question.
  • Zero credit: blank, or "I don't know" with no further attempt. Note: do not penalize students for "I don't know" — record it (for the diagnostic) but give them the same zero-cost-to-the-final-grade effect.

Total: 50 points. Pre-course exam is included in the grade, but the cut-points are calibrated so a student who took zero stats can earn ~25/50 with sensible guessing and partial English-prose answers.

Compute each student's pre/post gain as post_pct − pre_pct (in percentage points). The aggregate gain is the primary measure of course effectiveness; expect class median of +30 to +50 points.

Part A — Probability fundamentals (8 points)

A1. Conditional probability (2 pts)

Bayes: P(infected | +) = (0.92 × 0.05) / (0.92 × 0.05 + 0.05 × 0.95) = 0.046 / (0.046 + 0.0475) = 0.492 ≈ 0.49.

(0.92 * 0.05) / (0.92 * 0.05 + 0.05 * 0.95) # 0.4919

Scoring: 2 pts for correct ~0.49. 1 pt if they set up the Bayes formula but compute wrong. 0 if they answer "0.92" (interpret sensitivity as posterior).

A2. Independence vs mutually exclusive (2 pts)

Independent: knowing one tells you nothing about the other (P(A|B) = P(A)). Mutually exclusive: cannot both occur (P(A∩B) = 0). Example pairs vary.

Scoring: 1 pt for correct distinction; 1 pt for a sensible ecological example pair.

A3. Joint probability (2 pts)

P(≥1 detection) = 1 − (0.7)² = 0.51.

Scoring: 2 pts for 0.51. 1 pt if they answered 0.6 (added probabilities, ignoring intersection) or 0.09 (multiplied detections).

A4. Expected value (2 pts)

E[payoff] = 0.7 × \$10,000 + 0.3 × (−\$2,000) = \$7,000 − \$600 = \$6,400.

Scoring: 2 pts for \$6,400. 1 pt for correct formulation but arithmetic error.

Part B — Distributions (8 points)

B1. Pick the distribution (2 pts; ~0.67 pt each)

a. Binomial (n=12, p). b. Poisson. c. Multinomial.

B2. Mean and variance of Poisson(4) (2 pts)

Mean = 4, Variance = 4. They are equal — that is a defining feature of the Poisson and tends to fail in real ecological data (which are usually over-dispersed).

Scoring: 1 pt for mean = var = 4; 1 pt for noting equality as a feature (and/or its failure mode in real data).

B3. Continuous distribution choice (2 pts)

Acceptable: Lognormal (log scale is Normal), Gamma (variance scales with mean), or even Weibull. The "why" should mention positive support and right-skew accommodation.

B4. Normal probability (2 pts)

z = (160 − 152) / 8 = 1. P(Z > 1) ≈ 0.159 ≈ 16%.

Acceptable answers: "about 16%" or "approximately 15–18%". Full credit either way.

Part C — Regression (10 points)

C1. Reading a coefficient (2 pts)

"For each additional mm of length, average weight increases by 0.8 g." (Or equivalent.) Full credit for any version that correctly identifies "per unit predictor" and the direction.

C2. Choosing a GLM (3 pts)

a. Binomial(logit). b. Poisson(log) (or NB for overdispersion). c. Gamma(log) or Lognormal regression (or Normal on log y).

C3. Log-link interpretation (3 pts)

Multiplicative effect on expected response = exp(0.30) = 1.35 per unit increase in x. Equivalently, a ~35% increase per unit.

Scoring: 3 pts for the multiplicative reading. 1 pt for "the response goes up" with no quantification. 0 for "the response increases by 0.3."

C4. Logit-link interpretation (2 pts)

exp(−0.5) = 0.61. Each additional year of age multiplies the odds of survival by 0.61 (a 39% reduction in odds). Full credit for noting the multiplicative effect on odds (not probability).

Common partial-credit answer: "the probability decreases" — give 1 pt; this is correct in direction but misses the odds-ratio interpretation.

Part D — Mixed models / hierarchy (8 points)

D1. When to use a random effect (2 pts)

Plots within a site are not independent (share unmeasured site-level drivers — soil, microclimate, observer). Ignoring this leads to standard errors that are too narrow and inflated false-positive rates.

D2. Random vs fixed (3 pts)

a. Fixed (you care about males vs females specifically).

b. Random if you want to generalize across years; can also be modeled as fixed if you care about specific years.

c. Fixed (the experimental design specifies the levels).

D3. Shrinkage intuition (3 pts)

Group-level estimates are pulled toward the overall mean, with the pull being stronger for groups with fewer observations. This is desirable because small groups have noisy estimates; shrinkage trades a little bias for substantially reduced variance, producing better out-of-sample predictions.

Scoring: 3 pts for a description mentioning small-sample pull and predictive accuracy. 1–2 pts for partial intuition. 0 for "I don't know" — but record for the diagnostic.

Part E — Likelihood (5 points)

E1. What is a likelihood? (2 pts)

The probability of the observed data given a set of parameter values, viewed as a function of those parameters (with data fixed). A probability is the converse: a function of outcomes given parameters.

E2. MLE for a proportion (3 pts)

MLE = 30/50 = 0.60.

Acceptable derivations: (a) intuitive ("the sample proportion is the best estimator"), (b) algebraic from the Binomial likelihood (set derivative of log L = 0 and solve for p).

Scoring: 3 pts for 0.6 with any justification. 2 pts for 0.6 with no reasoning. 0 for incorrect.

Part F — Bayes (8 points)

F1. Prior, likelihood, posterior (2 pts)

Prior: belief about parameters before seeing the data. Likelihood: probability of the data given parameter values. Posterior: belief about parameters after seeing the data, obtained by combining prior and likelihood via Bayes' rule.

F2. Posterior interpretation (3 pts)

Bayesian: "Given the data and model, there is a 95% probability that the parameter lies in (0.2, 0.8)." Frequentist 95% CI: "If we repeated this experiment many times and computed CIs the same way each time, 95% of those intervals would contain the true parameter." The Bayesian statement is the more direct probability statement people usually want.

F3. Why MCMC (3 pts)

The posterior involves an integral in the denominator (marginal likelihood) that is intractable for most realistic models — no closed form exists. MCMC samples from the posterior without computing that integral, by constructing a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the posterior. The samples can then be summarized like draws from any distribution.

Scoring: 3 pts for mentioning the intractable normalizing integral and that MCMC samples rather than computes. 1–2 pts for "MCMC samples from the posterior" without the why. 0 for "I don't know" — record for the diagnostic.

Part G — Free response (3 points)

G1. Tell me about your data

Full credit (3 pts) for any reasonable description and question, even if statistically naive. This is a diagnostic for tuning workshop examples — record the dominant taxa, response variables, and aspirations for use in the early afternoon Day 1 discussion.

Aggregate analysis to do after grading.
  1. Compute per-student pre-course percent.
  2. After Day 5, compute per-student post-course percent (using the parallel post-course key).
  3. Compute gain = post − pre. Report mean, SD, and a paired-difference plot.
  4. Identify topics where the class collectively did worst on the pre-course exam — these guide where to put extra emphasis on subsequent runs of the workshop.
