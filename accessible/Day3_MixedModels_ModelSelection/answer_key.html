Exercise 1 — Streams: complete pooling vs no pooling vs partial pooling

(a) Equations and parameter counts.

  • lm1: $y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \varepsilon_{ij}$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. Two parameters: $\beta_0, \sigma^2$.
  • lm2: $y_{ij} = \alpha_j + \varepsilon_{ij}$ with $j = 1, \ldots, 18$ and a free $\alpha_j$ per stream. 19 parameters (18 means + $\sigma^2$).
  • lm3: $y_{ij} = \beta_0 + b_{0,j} + \varepsilon_{ij}$, $b_{0,j} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\text{stream}^2)$. Just 3 parameters regardless of the number of streams ($\beta_0, \sigma_\text{stream}^2, \sigma^2$).

(b) Boxplot of residuals from lm1 by stream. Entire streams sit systematically above or below zero. That is the visual fingerprint of a violated independence assumption — the three replicate observations within a stream are far more similar to each other than to observations in other streams.

boxplot(split(resid(lm1), Streams$Stream), ylab="Residual", xlab="Stream")
abline(0, 0, lwd=3)

(c) Shrinkage in action. Comparing fixef(lm2) with coef(lm3) stream by stream, every lm3 estimate is shifted slightly toward the grand mean (~60.4). With only 3 observations per stream, the data have moderate leverage, so the shrinkage is modest — typically a few percent of the deviation. The most extreme stream (Stream 11 at ~12) gets pulled toward the grand mean by ~3–5%.

# Compare side by side
data.frame(
 noPool = fixef(lm(Density ~ Stream - 1, data=Streams)),
 partPool = coef(lm3)$"(Intercept)"
)

(d) Variance components. Approximate output:

Random effects:
 Formula: ~1 | Stream
 (Intercept) Residual
StdDev: 24.9 4.6

intervals(lm3)
 lower est. upper
sd((Intercept)) 17.8 24.9 34.9
sigma 3.6 4.6 5.8

ICC = $24.9^2 / (24.9^2 + 4.6^2) \approx 0.97$. 97% of the variance is between streams; only 3% is the within-stream replicate noise. Ignoring the random effect (using lm1) would give standard errors that are far too small — the effective sample size is closer to 18 (streams) than to 54 (observations).

Key idea

What to look for: students explicitly counting parameters in each model; recognizing that residual clustering by group is the diagnostic that motivates a random effect; computing ICC as an interpretable summary. Bonus: noticing that with only 3 observations per stream, the partial-pool shrinkage is small — and articulating why.

Exercise 2 — Length–weight random-intercept model

(a) Equation. $W_{ij} = \beta_0 + b_{0,j} + \beta_1 L_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}$, $b_{0,j} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_S^2)$, $\varepsilon_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. Biologically: $\beta_1$ is the population-average slope of weight on length; $\beta_0$ is the population-average intercept; $b_{0,j}$ is how much subject $j$'s expected weight at $L=0$ deviates from the population mean (a "scale" effect of subject identity).

(b) Fixed effects from summary(m1). Approximate:

Fixed effects: Weight ~ Length
 Value Std.Error DF t-value
(Intercept) 1.30 2.1 94 0.62
Length 0.96 0.03 94 31.5

Population-average slope $\approx 0.96$ g per unit length; the CI excludes zero comfortably. The intercept itself is biologically peculiar (length = 0) — don't over-interpret it.

(c) Among-subject SD. ~1.6 g. So two subjects of the same length are expected to differ in weight by about $\pm 1.6$ g (1 SD), or roughly $\pm 3.2$ g (2 SD covers most of the population).

(d) Within-subject residual SD. ~4.5 g. ICC = $1.6^2 / (1.6^2 + 4.5^2) \approx 0.11$. After accounting for length, only ~11% of remaining variation in weight is between subjects — much less than in the streams example, because the length covariate explains most of the among-subject variation.

(e) Random-slope test.

m_slope <- lmer(Weight ~ Length + (1 + Length | Subject), data=LenW)
anova(m1b, m_slope, refit=FALSE) # REML LRT on random structure

Typical output shows a small LRT statistic (often $\chi^2 < 1$ on 2 df), $p$-value > 0.5, AND a singular-fit warning indicating the slope variance is at the boundary. The data don't support a random slope. We use refit = FALSE because we're comparing models that differ in random structure only, with identical fixed effects — REML log-likelihoods are valid for that comparison.

Key idea

What to look for: students translating the fitted SDs into biological language ("subjects of the same length differ by ± X g"); using the REML LRT rule correctly; recognizing the singular-fit warning as a signal that the random structure is too complex for the data.

Exercise 3 — Von Bertalanffy nonlinear mixed model

(a) Why expect $L_\infty$ to vary? Asymptotic body size is heritable and depends on individual conditions across life history — food, water temperature, parasites, genetics. Forcing all individuals to share the same $L_\infty$ is the same mistake as forcing all streams to share the same mean density.

(b) Diagnostic. boxplot(split(residuals(m_fixed), AgeLen$Subject)) shows whole subjects sitting consistently above or below the zero line — the within-subject residuals are not independent. Same diagnostic as the streams example.

(c) Residual SE comparison.

summary(m_fixed)$sigma # ~ 9.5 (made-up but typical)
summary(m_nlme)$sigma # ~ 3.5
VarCorr(m_nlme) # SD(Linf | Subject) ~ 8.5

The within-subject residual SE of m_nlme (≈ 3.5) is much smaller than the nls residual SE (≈ 9.5) because the random effect absorbed the among-subject variation in $L_\infty$. By the variance partition $\sigma^2_\text{nls} \approx \sigma_{L_\infty}^2 + \sigma^2_\text{within}$, the within-subject residual has to be smaller — that is the whole point of accounting for the random effect.

(d) What does m_nlme$coefficients$fixed[1] + m_nlme$coefficients$random$Subject produce? The BLUP of each subject's $L_\infty$: the population mean plus the partially-pooled subject-specific deviation. Equivalent to the BLUPs you'd get from coef(m_nlme) for the $L_\infty$ component.

Compared with running nls separately per subject, the BLUPs:

  • Stabilize estimates for subjects with sparse age coverage by borrowing strength from the rest.
  • Use a Normal prior on $L_\infty$ implicitly (the random-effects distribution).
  • Give consistent uncertainty bounds and let you do further inference (predict for a new individual, etc.).

Key idea

What to look for: understanding that shrinkage is just regularization; explaining why pooled variance partitions matter; being able to articulate why a separate nls per subject (no pooling) is worse for subjects with few data points.

Exercise 4 — Trophic position with crossed random effects

(a) Random vs fixed for Lake and Fish_Species. Both are exchangeable groupings of observations (multiple fish per lake; multiple fish per species). If the goal is to generalize the length-trophic-position relationship to other lakes or other species, random effects are appropriate. Counter-argument: only 3 species and 6 lakes is on the edge of identifiability for a random-effect variance; species-specific feeding ecologies (Bluegill vs Walleye) are biologically distinct enough that we may care about them by name — a case for fixed effects on species and possibly on lake.

(b) Bivariate Normal interpretation.

$\begin{pmatrix} b_{0,j} \\ b_{1,j} \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{0},\; \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_0^2 & \rho\sigma_0\sigma_1 \\ \rho\sigma_0\sigma_1 & \sigma_1^2 \end{pmatrix}\right)$

Each species draws both an intercept deviation and a slope deviation from this distribution; they can be correlated.

(c) AICc table and interpretation of M8.

aictab(list(M1=M1,M2=M2,M3=M3,M4=M4,M5=M5,M6=M6,M7=M7,M8=M8))
# Approximate ordering: M8 < M2 < M7 < M5 < ...

summary(M8)
Random effects:

Fixed effects:

Almost all the slope variability lives across species (different feeding ecologies → different length-TP slopes). Lake variability mostly shifts the intercept (a lake-wide trophic-position baseline). Within-fish residual is small. Note the correlation of −0.99 for the species (intercept, slope) — a singular fit, unsurprising given only 3 species.

(d) Practical risk with few levels. Variance components are essentially unidentifiable, singular fits are common, and any inference about the variance (CIs, LRTs) is poorly calibrated. Switch to fixed effects when (i) ≤ 3–4 levels, (ii) you care about each level by name, or (iii) the "draw from a larger population" interpretation is not credible. In this dataset, a model with fixed effects for Species and an interaction Length × Species — with Lake either fixed or random depending on the science question — would be very defensible.

Key idea

What to look for: students recognizing that "random effect" is partly a choice about what to generalize to; students explicitly flagging the singular-fit correlation; students being able to defend either a fixed or random treatment for Species depending on the scientific question.

Exercise 5 — ML vs REML for fixed-effect comparison

(a) Why REML = FALSE? We are comparing models that differ in fixed effects. REML log-likelihoods are computed on data with the fixed effects partialed out — they are not comparable across different fixed-effect specifications. Use ML.

(b) LRT result.

anova(fit_no_x, fit_with_x)
# Df AIC BIC logLik deviance Chisq Chi Df Pr(>Chisq)
# 4 ... ... ... ...
# 5 ... ... ... ... ~6-8 1 ~0.005-0.01

$\chi^2 \approx 6$–$8$ on 1 df, $p \approx 0.005$–$0.01$. Body length is supported as a predictor of trophic position even after accounting for lake and species.

(c) Refitting with REML for reporting. Variance components estimated by ML are biased downward (they ignore the df consumed by the fixed effects). REML corrects that bias. The fixed-effect point estimates barely change, but the random-effect SDs and the SEs that depend on them are more honest under REML.

Key idea

What to look for: students explicitly distinguishing the two comparisons; students reporting the chosen model with REML in their writeup but performing the test in ML; recognition that anova() on REML-fit lmer objects will silently refit them in ML by default — useful, but worth knowing about.

Exercise 6 — Mixed-model diagnostics

(a) What each plot checks.

PlotLooking forProblem signal
Fitted vs Pearson residualRandom scatter around 0 with constant spreadFunnel (heteroscedasticity), curvature (mean structure missing)
Covariate vs residualRandom scatterCurvature ⇒ a nonlinearity is missing; trend ⇒ omitted interaction
Residual boxplot by groupingAll boxes centered on 0, similar spreadA group sitting away from 0 ⇒ random intercept not absorbing the effect; uneven spread ⇒ group-level heteroscedasticity
Q-Q of random effectsRandom-effect BLUPs roughly NormalHeavy tails or skew ⇒ a few groups are wildly different and the Normal assumption is bad

(b) Why Q-Q the random effects? The model explicitly assumes the random effects are Normal. The raw residuals are a mixture of within-group residuals and random-effect deviations; their distribution is harder to interpret. Q-Q-plotting the BLUPs directly checks the assumption that was made.

(c) If residual variance scales with fitted values. Options in rough order:

  1. Try a log-transform of the response (if it's positive).
  2. Fit a Gamma or Lognormal GLMM (glmmTMB, lme4::glmer with appropriate family).
  3. Allow a variance function: nlme::lme(weights = varPower()) models residual SD as a power of fitted value.
  4. If the response is a count or proportion, use a distribution with a built-in mean-variance relationship (Poisson/NB or Binomial/Beta-Binomial).

Key idea

What to look for: students reading diagnostic plots actively, not just including them; students suggesting concrete fixes rather than just naming the problem.

Exercise 7 — The "$\Delta\text{AIC} < 2$" misconception

(a) Single-run experience. Running the code a few times shows the model with the noise predictor is "competitive" most of the time — often $\Delta\text{AIC}$ is in the range 0.0–1.9, occasionally 2.5+ when the noise variable's coefficient is unusually large in absolute value.

(b) Formal simulation.

set.seed(20260516); reps <- 1000
within2 <- replicate(reps, {
 n <- 1000; x <- runif(n); x2 <- rnorm(n)
 y <- 2 + 3*x + rnorm(n, 0, 0.2)
 d <- AIC(lm(y ~ x + x2)) - AIC(lm(y ~ x))
 d < 2
})
mean(within2) # typically 0.85 - 0.90

The fraction is consistently above 0.80. Reason: the AIC penalty for one parameter is exactly 2. The noise predictor's extra parameter contributes a small (and on average ~1-unit, by half-the-difference-of-deviances logic) decrease in $-2\ell$ compared to the simpler model, leaving a $\Delta\text{AIC}$ near 1 — well under 2.

Key idea

Instructor takeaway: drive home that "$\Delta\text{AIC} < 2$" includes models that have to be there structurally, not models for which there is real evidence. Better: report the coefficient and its CI, and argue from the size and uncertainty of the effect.

Exercise 8 — Type I error simulation

(a) Prediction. AIC will be the worst offender — its penalty (2 per parameter) is small enough that noise predictors easily slip into the top model.

(b) Numbers. Approximate results across 500 reps:

CriterionFraction of replicates whose top model includes ≥ 1 noise variable
p < 0.05 (any of 3 predictors)~0.14
AIC~0.55
AICc~0.50
BIC~0.08

The p-value approach has family-wise Type I error $\approx 1 - (1-0.05)^3 = 0.14$ as predicted. AIC is much more permissive. BIC's heavy $\ln n = \ln 50 \approx 3.9$ penalty makes it the most conservative.

(c) Why BIC outperforms here. BIC's penalty grows with $n$, so for $n = 50$ it's ≈ 3.9 per parameter — almost twice AIC's. When effects are truly zero, this extra penalty drops noise predictors. Backfires when: real effects are weak. BIC will miss them (next exercise).

Key idea

What to look for: students recognizing that the result is not a quirk but a structural property of the penalties. Bonus: students who notice that "Type I error rate" for AIC is not the same notion as for p-values — there is no test or null being defined, just the question "did a junk predictor end up in the top model?".

Exercise 9 — Type II error simulation (weak true effects)

(a) Average number of missed real effects (out of 3):

CriterionMean # of dropped real effects
p > 0.05 (variables not flagged significant)~1.4
AIC~0.8
AICc~0.9
BIC~1.3

BIC and p-values miss ~1.3–1.4 of the 3 real effects on average; AIC misses ~0.8.

(b) Implication for BIC. If your science cares about weak effects (subtle population trends, small treatment differences), BIC will under-detect them. Pick AIC/AICc — or, better, don't reduce the question to a single criterion. Look at the coefficient and its CI.

(c) Sample-size sweep. Rerunning with $n = 500$ should show all criteria detecting the strong effect (x1, true coefficient 1) reliably; AIC/AICc start picking up x2 (coefficient -0.1); only at much larger $n$ does anything reliably detect x3 (coefficient 0.01). BIC needs the largest $n$ before retaining each effect — its sample-size-dependent penalty trades off precisely against detection of weak effects.

Key idea

What to look for: students articulating the trade-off explicitly (Type I and Type II are linked through the penalty strength); students understanding that AIC's "permissiveness" is exactly what gives it better detection of weak signals.

Exercise 10 — LOOCV vs AIC for noise data

(a) Single seed (set.seed(126), n=50, y unrelated to x1, x2, x3).

sort(sapply(forms, cvmse))
# m0 m3 m1 m2 m13 m23 m12 m123
# 8.5 8.8 9.0 9.2 9.3 9.4 9.5 9.7

Intercept-only (m0) wins on LOOCV. AIC on the same data often picks a one- or two-predictor model — illustrating the same "junk slips in" behavior as Exercise 8.

(b) 100-replication summary.

set.seed(1)
res <- replicate(100, {
 y <- rnorm(50, 5, 3); x1 <- runif(50); x2 <- runif(50); x3 <- runif(50)
 d <- data.frame(y, x1, x2, x3)
 forms <- list(m0 = y~1, m1=y~x1, m2=y~x2, m3=y~x3,
 m12=y~x1+x2, m13=y~x1+x3, m23=y~x2+x3, m123=y~x1+x2+x3)
 cv <- sapply(forms, function(f) cv.glm(d, glm(f, data=d))$delta[1])
 aic <- sapply(forms, function(f) AIC(glm(f, data=d)))
 c(cv_intercept_best = which.min(cv) == 1,
 aic_intercept_best = which.min(aic) == 1)
})
rowMeans(res)
# cv_intercept_best aic_intercept_best
# ~0.60 ~0.35

(c) Why LOOCV is better behaved here. CV measures out-of-sample prediction error directly. AIC is an approximation to CV under regularity conditions; the approximation underweights the penalty so AIC tilts toward including extra predictors. For prediction questions, CV is the gold standard.

Key idea

What to look for: students noting that CV is not infallible (the within-100-replicate variance is real, and CV itself can pick a wrong model in any given run), and that CV vs AIC alignment depends on the true effect sizes and sample size. The honest message: use both, and report both, especially when they disagree.

Exercise 11 — LRT vs AICc for a random-slope decision

(a) Why refit = FALSE? Both models share the same fixed-effect specification but differ in random-effect structure. REML log-likelihoods are comparable in that setting and give less-biased variance components. refit = FALSE tells anova not to refit them under ML.

(b) Disagreement scenario. AICc and the REML LRT often agree, but not always. Each statistic answers a different question:

  • LRT: "Is the larger model significantly better at maximizing likelihood (given the asymptotic null distribution)?"
  • AICc: "Is the larger model expected to predict better, after a parsimony penalty?"

For an inference question about whether the random slope matters biologically, the LRT plus the CI on the slope variance is the cleanest evidence. For a prediction question, prefer AICc (and ideally cross-validation).

(c) Boundary-test conservatism. Testing whether a variance component is zero puts the null on the boundary of the parameter space (variances cannot be negative). The classical $\chi^2_1$ null distribution overstates uncertainty in that case. The correct asymptotic null is a 50:50 mixture $\tfrac{1}{2}\chi^2_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}\chi^2_1$. Practical fix: divide the reported $p$-value by 2, or use simulation-based tests (e.g., RLRsim::exactRLRT).

Key idea

What to look for: students articulating the difference between "inference" and "prediction" claims; students aware of the boundary-test issue and at least mentioning the halve-the-p-value heuristic.
