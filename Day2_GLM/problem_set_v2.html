Problem 1 · continuous distribution choice

Tree DBH on a 1-hectare plot

You map every stem > 5 cm DBH on a hectare of mixed Douglas-fir / western hemlock forest. The empirical DBH distribution is strongly right-skewed: many small understory stems, a few enormous overstory trees, with a long right tail.

  1. Which of {Normal, Lognormal, Gamma, Exponential, Beta} would you choose for DBH? Why?
  2. If $\log(\text{DBH}) \sim \mathcal{N}(3.0, 0.6^2)$, what is the expected DBH on the raw scale? (Hint: lognormal mean is $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$.)
  3. What is the median DBH? Why does it differ from the mean?
Problem 2 · Normal vs lognormal regression

Aboveground biomass vs basal area

For 60 forested plots you have aboveground biomass (Mg/ha) and basal area (m²/ha). Biomass spans 5 to 800 Mg/ha. Two analysts fit:

  • Analyst A: lm(biomass ~ basal_area)
  • Analyst B: lm(log(biomass) ~ log(basal_area))
  1. Why is Analyst A likely to get heteroskedastic residuals?
  2. If Analyst B's slope is $\hat\beta_1 = 1.10$, what does this mean ecologically? (Power law.)
  3. What multiplicative effect on biomass corresponds to a doubling of basal area, under Analyst B's model?
  4. If you wanted Analyst A's identity-link mean of biomass at a basal area of 40 m²/ha, what is wrong with simply exponentiating $\hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 \log(40)$?
Problem 3 · Poisson regression, log-link interpretation

Black-throated gray warbler counts on point counts

From 120 ten-minute point counts in oak woodland, you fit

glm(warblers ~ canopy_oak + elev_km, family = poisson, data = pc)

and get $\hat\beta_{\text{oak}} = +0.020$ per percent canopy oak, $\hat\beta_{\text{elev}} = -0.55$ per km elevation. Intercept $\hat\beta_0 = +0.10$.

  1. What is the predicted mean count at oak = 0% canopy, elev = 0 km?
  2. What is the multiplicative effect of going from 10% to 60% oak canopy?
  3. What is the elevation half-decay (elevation at which expected count is half the sea-level expectation)?
  4. Write the one-sentence biology summary you would put in a manuscript.
Problem 4 · binomial logistic, logit interpretation

Nest success of song sparrow vs nest height

250 monitored song-sparrow nests; outcome is fledged (1) or failed (0). Predictor: nest height in cm. Logistic regression returns intercept $-0.50$ and slope $+0.025$ per cm.

  1. What is the predicted probability of success at the ground (height = 0)?
  2. At what height does predicted probability equal 0.5?
  3. What is the odds ratio for a 100 cm increase in nest height? Translate to plain English.
  4. Use the divide-by-4 rule to estimate the per-cm probability change at the inflection point. Compare to the formal marginal effect $\beta p (1-p)$.
Problem 5 · NB vs Poisson

Acorn mast counts from individual oaks

You collect acorn counts from 80 individual white oaks over one fall. Mean = 350, variance = 18,000. Both fits use a single intercept (no predictors).

  1. What is the dispersion ratio? Does the Poisson assumption hold?
  2. Find the NB size parameter $k$ such that variance = $\mu + \mu^2/k$ matches the data.
  3. Simulate 1000 oaks under both Poisson(350) and NB($\mu = 350, k$). Compare P(count = 0) and P(count > 1500).
  4. What is the biological mechanism producing this overdispersion in mast counts?
Problem 6 · interaction

Stand age × management interaction on bird species richness

Forty-eight stands; species richness (a count) modeled as Poisson with predictors stand age (years, continuous) and management (factor: unmanaged, thinned). Fit

glm(richness ~ age * management, family = poisson, data = stands)

Coefficients: intercept = 1.50, age = +0.005, managementThinned = +0.30, age:managementThinned = $-0.010$.

  1. What is the age slope on unmanaged stands? Translate to a per-decade percent change.
  2. What is the age slope on thinned stands?
  3. At age 0, what is the multiplicative effect of thinning?
  4. At what stand age do thinned and unmanaged stands have equal expected richness? Interpret this crossover biologically.
Problem 7 · offsets

Detection rate of pileated woodpeckers — variable point-count duration

Across 90 stations, point counts ranged from 5 to 25 minutes (not all the same). You record total detections per station. Two analysts:

  • Analyst A: glm(detections ~ forest_age, family = poisson)
  • Analyst B: glm(detections ~ forest_age + offset(log(minutes)), family = poisson)
  1. What is Analyst B's intercept reporting that Analyst A's intercept is not?
  2. Why is the offset on the log scale, not the raw scale?
  3. If Analyst B's forest_age coefficient is $+0.04$ per decade, write the one-sentence biology statement.
  4. What kind of bias would Analyst A introduce if longer counts happened to be conducted preferentially in old-growth stands?
Problem 8 · probability ↔ odds ↔ log-odds

Northern spotted owl occupancy from canopy cover

Logistic regression of site-occupancy on percent canopy cover. Intercept = $-2.0$, slope = $+0.05$ per percent canopy.

  1. Compute fitted $p$ at canopy = 0, 40, 70, and 100%.
  2. Express the slope as an odds ratio per 1% canopy and per 20% canopy.
  3. For a manager comparing a 40% canopy stand to a 70% canopy stand, what is the difference in occupancy probability?
  4. Why is the same 30-point canopy change worth more "probability" near 40% than near 90%?
Problem 9 · back-transform with CI

Cone production vs crown class

A Gamma GLM with log link gives, for the contrast "dominant vs codominant trees", a coefficient $\hat\beta = +0.55$ with 95% CI $(0.32, 0.78)$ on the log scale.

  1. Back-transform the point estimate. What multiplicative effect does that represent?
  2. Back-transform the CI. Is it symmetric?
  3. Why is exponentiating each endpoint correct, and exponentiating the midpoint wrong?
  4. One-sentence biology, suitable for a results section.
Problem 10 · logistic threshold + marginal effects

Hermit thrush habitat threshold

Presence/absence of hermit thrush at 300 sites. Model: glm(present ~ basal_area + shrub_cover, family = binomial). Intercept $-3.0$; basal-area slope $+0.08$ per m²/ha; shrub slope $+0.04$ per percent shrub.

  1. For a site with shrub cover = 25%, at what basal area does predicted occupancy probability equal 0.5?
  2. For a site with shrub cover = 50%, what is the new "threshold" basal area?
  3. Compute the marginal effect of basal area on probability at the 0.5 inflection.
  4. If a manager can either raise basal area by 5 m²/ha or shrub cover by 10 percentage points, which intervention has the larger predicted effect at a site currently at $p = 0.3$?
