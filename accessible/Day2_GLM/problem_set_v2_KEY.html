Day 2 · Alternate Problem Set v2

Forest and songbird themed problems

Ten novel problems covering continuous distribution choice, Normal vs lognormal regression, Poisson with log-link interpretation, logistic regression with odds-ratio interpretation, NB vs Poisson, interaction terms, and offsets — all set in temperate-forest and songbird-ecology systems. Heavy emphasis on translating coefficients into biology you would put in a manuscript.

Work each problem before opening the answer. The biological-interpretation sentence is the assessment, not the numeric output. Every answer below ends with a "say it as biology" line — use those as a checklist of how to communicate.

Problem 1 · continuous distribution choice

Tree DBH on a 1-hectare plot

You map every stem > 5 cm DBH on a hectare of mixed Douglas-fir / western hemlock forest. The empirical DBH distribution is strongly right-skewed: many small understory stems, a few enormous overstory trees, with a long right tail.

  1. Which of {Normal, Lognormal, Gamma, Exponential, Beta} would you choose for DBH? Why?
  2. If $\log(\text{DBH}) \sim \mathcal{N}(3.0, 0.6^2)$, what is the expected DBH on the raw scale? (Hint: lognormal mean is $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$.)
  3. What is the median DBH? Why does it differ from the mean?
Problem 2 · Normal vs lognormal regression

Aboveground biomass vs basal area

For 60 forested plots you have aboveground biomass (Mg/ha) and basal area (m²/ha). Biomass spans 5 to 800 Mg/ha. Two analysts fit:

  • Analyst A: lm(biomass ~ basal_area)
  • Analyst B: lm(log(biomass) ~ log(basal_area))
  1. Why is Analyst A likely to get heteroskedastic residuals?
  2. If Analyst B's slope is $\hat\beta_1 = 1.10$, what does this mean ecologically? (Power law.)
  3. What multiplicative effect on biomass corresponds to a doubling of basal area, under Analyst B's model?
  4. If you wanted Analyst A's identity-link mean of biomass at a basal area of 40 m²/ha, what is wrong with simply exponentiating $\hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 \log(40)$?
Problem 3 · Poisson regression, log-link interpretation

Black-throated gray warbler counts on point counts

From 120 ten-minute point counts in oak woodland, you fit

glm(warblers ~ canopy_oak + elev_km, family = poisson, data = pc)

and get $\hat\beta_{\text{oak}} = +0.020$ per percent canopy oak, $\hat\beta_{\text{elev}} = -0.55$ per km elevation. Intercept $\hat\beta_0 = +0.10$.

  1. What is the predicted mean count at oak = 0% canopy, elev = 0 km?
  2. What is the multiplicative effect of going from 10% to 60% oak canopy?
  3. What is the elevation half-decay (elevation at which expected count is half the sea-level expectation)?
  4. Write the one-sentence biology summary you would put in a manuscript.
Problem 4 · binomial logistic, logit interpretation

Nest success of song sparrow vs nest height

250 monitored song-sparrow nests; outcome is fledged (1) or failed (0). Predictor: nest height in cm. Logistic regression returns intercept $-0.50$ and slope $+0.025$ per cm.

  1. What is the predicted probability of success at the ground (height = 0)?
  2. At what height does predicted probability equal 0.5?
  3. What is the odds ratio for a 100 cm increase in nest height? Translate to plain English.
  4. Use the divide-by-4 rule to estimate the per-cm probability change at the inflection point. Compare to the formal marginal effect $\beta p (1-p)$.
Problem 5 · NB vs Poisson

Acorn mast counts from individual oaks

You collect acorn counts from 80 individual white oaks over one fall. Mean = 350, variance = 18,000. Both fits use a single intercept (no predictors).

  1. What is the dispersion ratio? Does the Poisson assumption hold?
  2. Find the NB size parameter $k$ such that variance = $\mu + \mu^2/k$ matches the data.
  3. Simulate 1000 oaks under both Poisson(350) and NB($\mu = 350, k$). Compare P(count = 0) and P(count > 1500).
  4. What is the biological mechanism producing this overdispersion in mast counts?
Problem 6 · interaction

Stand age × management interaction on bird species richness

Forty-eight stands; species richness (a count) modeled as Poisson with predictors stand age (years, continuous) and management (factor: unmanaged, thinned). Fit

glm(richness ~ age * management, family = poisson, data = stands)

Coefficients: intercept = 1.50, age = +0.005, managementThinned = +0.30, age:managementThinned = $-0.010$.

  1. What is the age slope on unmanaged stands? Translate to a per-decade percent change.
  2. What is the age slope on thinned stands?
  3. At age 0, what is the multiplicative effect of thinning?
  4. At what stand age do thinned and unmanaged stands have equal expected richness? Interpret this crossover biologically.
Problem 7 · offsets

Detection rate of pileated woodpeckers — variable point-count duration

Across 90 stations, point counts ranged from 5 to 25 minutes (not all the same). You record total detections per station. Two analysts:

  • Analyst A: glm(detections ~ forest_age, family = poisson)
  • Analyst B: glm(detections ~ forest_age + offset(log(minutes)), family = poisson)
  1. What is Analyst B's intercept reporting that Analyst A's intercept is not?
  2. Why is the offset on the log scale, not the raw scale?
  3. If Analyst B's forest_age coefficient is $+0.04$ per decade, write the one-sentence biology statement.
  4. What kind of bias would Analyst A introduce if longer counts happened to be conducted preferentially in old-growth stands?
Problem 8 · probability ↔ odds ↔ log-odds

Northern spotted owl occupancy from canopy cover

Logistic regression of site-occupancy on percent canopy cover. Intercept = $-2.0$, slope = $+0.05$ per percent canopy.

  1. Compute fitted $p$ at canopy = 0, 40, 70, and 100%.
  2. Express the slope as an odds ratio per 1% canopy and per 20% canopy.
  3. For a manager comparing a 40% canopy stand to a 70% canopy stand, what is the difference in occupancy probability?
  4. Why is the same 30-point canopy change worth more "probability" near 40% than near 90%?
Problem 9 · back-transform with CI

Cone production vs crown class

A Gamma GLM with log link gives, for the contrast "dominant vs codominant trees", a coefficient $\hat\beta = +0.55$ with 95% CI $(0.32, 0.78)$ on the log scale.

  1. Back-transform the point estimate. What multiplicative effect does that represent?
  2. Back-transform the CI. Is it symmetric?
  3. Why is exponentiating each endpoint correct, and exponentiating the midpoint wrong?
  4. One-sentence biology, suitable for a results section.
Problem 10 · logistic threshold + marginal effects

Hermit thrush habitat threshold

Presence/absence of hermit thrush at 300 sites. Model: glm(present ~ basal_area + shrub_cover, family = binomial). Intercept $-3.0$; basal-area slope $+0.08$ per m²/ha; shrub slope $+0.04$ per percent shrub.

  1. For a site with shrub cover = 25%, at what basal area does predicted occupancy probability equal 0.5?
  2. For a site with shrub cover = 50%, what is the new "threshold" basal area?
  3. Compute the marginal effect of basal area on probability at the 0.5 inflection.
  4. If a manager can either raise basal area by 5 m²/ha or shrub cover by 10 percentage points, which intervention has the larger predicted effect at a site currently at $p = 0.3$?

Answers (with R)

Problem 1 — Tree DBH distribution

(a) Lognormal is the natural choice: stems are strictly positive, the distribution is right-skewed, and the data-generating process (growth = product of many small annual proportional increments) is exactly the lognormal story. Gamma is a viable second choice but lacks the multiplicative-growth motivation.

(b) Mean = $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2} = e^{3.0 + 0.18} = e^{3.18} = 24.0$ cm DBH.

(c) Median = $e^{\mu} = e^{3} = 20.1$ cm. The mean > median because the distribution is right-skewed; a few giant overstory trees pull the mean up.

mu <- 3.0; sig <- 0.6
exp(mu + sig^2/2) # 24.05
exp(mu) # 20.09
set.seed(1)
hist(rlnorm(1e4, mu, sig), breaks = 100, xlab = "DBH (cm)")

Say it as biology: "The DBH distribution is lognormal because tree size is the cumulative product of many proportional growth increments. The mean DBH (24 cm) exceeds the median (20 cm) by about 20% — a reminder that average-DBH summaries overstate the size of a typical stem in a structurally diverse forest."

Problem 2 — Normal vs lognormal regression

(a) Heteroskedasticity is the natural consequence of multiplicative processes. Biomass varies by 2 orders of magnitude across plots; under a Normal model, the residual SD is forced to be constant — so heavy plots get residuals near $\pm 100$ Mg/ha and light plots get residuals that could not possibly be that large. Residuals vs fitted will form the classic megaphone.

(b) Slope of 1.10 on the log-log scale means biomass scales as basal_area$^{1.10}$ — a near-isometric power law, with very slightly greater-than-proportional scaling (taller-on-average trees in stands with high basal area carry disproportionately more biomass).

(c) Doubling basal area: $e^{1.10 \cdot \ln 2} = 2^{1.10} = 2.14$. Doubling basal area is associated with a 2.14× increase in expected biomass.

(d) Exponentiating $\hat\beta_0 + \hat\beta_1 \log(40)$ gives the median biomass on the raw scale, not the mean. The mean on the raw scale is $\exp(\hat\mu + \hat\sigma^2/2)$ — you need to add the half-variance correction (the "Duan smearing" or lognormal mean adjustment).

# suppose hat_beta0 = -1.5, hat_beta1 = 1.10, sigma^2 = 0.3
exp(-1.5 + 1.10*log(40)) # 137 (= median biomass)
exp(-1.5 + 1.10*log(40) + 0.3/2) # 159 (= mean biomass)

Say it as biology: "Biomass scales as basal_area1.10 — a power law with near-isometric exponent — so a doubling of basal area roughly doubles biomass. Use the lognormal mean correction when predicting on the raw scale; the back-transformed line gives the median, not the mean."

Problem 3 — Warbler counts (Poisson, log link)

(a) $\exp(0.10) = 1.11$ warblers expected.

(b) $\exp(0.020 \cdot 50) = \exp(1.0) = 2.72$ — going from 10 to 60% oak canopy multiplies expected count by 2.7.

(c) Half-decay elevation: $-\ln 2 / -0.55 = 1.26$ km.

exp(0.10) # 1.105
exp(0.020 * 50) # 2.718
-log(2) / -0.55 # 1.26 km

(d) Say it as biology: "Black-throated gray warbler abundance on point counts increased ~2.7× as oak canopy cover increased from 10% to 60%, and fell by half for every 1.26 km of elevation — patterns consistent with this species' oak-savanna affinity and lowland distribution."

Problem 4 — Song sparrow nest success (logit)

(a) $\text{plogis}(-0.50) = 0.378$. Ground nests have ~38% predicted success.

(b) Solve $-0.50 + 0.025 h = 0 \Rightarrow h = 20$ cm.

(c) Odds ratio per 100 cm = $\exp(0.025 \cdot 100) = \exp(2.5) = 12.2$. Each 1-m rise in nest height multiplies the odds of success by ~12 — a dramatic predator-avoidance effect.

(d) Divide-by-4: $0.025/4 = 0.0063$ probability per cm at the inflection. Formal: $\beta p(1-p) = 0.025 \cdot 0.5 \cdot 0.5 = 0.00625$. Matches.

plogis(-0.50) # 0.378
-(-0.50)/0.025 # 20 cm
exp(0.025 * 100) # 12.18
0.025 / 4; 0.025 * 0.5 * 0.5 # 0.00625 0.00625

Say it as biology: "Nest height was a strong predictor of song-sparrow nest survival: odds of fledging rose 12-fold for every 1 m of nest elevation, with the 50% success threshold at ~20 cm. The pattern is consistent with ground-predator avoidance in shrub-nesting passerines."

Problem 5 — Acorn masts: NB vs Poisson

(a) Dispersion ratio = 18,000 / 350 = 51. Poisson is wildly inappropriate.

(b) $\mu + \mu^2/k = 18000 \Rightarrow 350 + 122500/k = 18000 \Rightarrow k = 122500/17650 = 6.94$.

set.seed(1); n <- 1000
pois <- rpois(n, 350)
nb <- rnbinom(n, mu = 350, size = 6.94)
mean(pois == 0); mean(nb == 0) # ~ 0 vs ~ 0.001 (very few)
mean(pois > 1500); mean(nb > 1500) # ~ 0 vs ~ 0.05
var(pois); var(nb) # ~ 350 vs ~ 18000

(c) Poisson assigns essentially zero probability to crops > 1500 acorns; NB puts about 5% there — matching the long upper tail observed in real mast data.

(d) Biological mechanism. Individual oaks vary enormously in vigor, age, and crown size, and mast years synchronize at the population level (weather-cued masting). Both forces add gamma-like variation onto a Poisson production process — exactly the NB story. The "mast" pattern of years with near-zero production interspersed with bumper crops is the population-level analog.

Say it as biology: "Per-tree acorn counts are over-dispersed roughly 50× relative to Poisson, consistent with strong individual-tree heterogeneity in mast production. A negative binomial with $k \approx 7$ captured the long upper tail of bumper-mast trees that the Poisson model placed essentially impossible probability on."

Problem 6 — Stand age × management interaction

(a) Unmanaged slope = $+0.005$ /yr. Per-decade multiplicative effect: $e^{0.05} = 1.051$ → about a 5% increase in expected richness per decade.

(b) Thinned slope = $0.005 - 0.010 = -0.005$ /yr → $e^{-0.05} = 0.951$ → a 5% decrease per decade.

(c) Effect of thinning at age 0: $e^{0.30} = 1.35$ → thinning raises expected richness by 35% in very young stands.

(d) Crossover where the management effect = 0: $0.30 + (-0.010) \cdot \text{age} = 0 \Rightarrow$ age = 30 yrs.

exp(0.005 * 10) # 1.051
exp(-0.005 * 10) # 0.951
exp(0.30) # 1.350
0.30 / 0.010 # 30 yrs

Say it as biology: "Thinning boosts bird species richness in young stands by ~35% but the effect erodes with stand age; by ~30 years, unmanaged stands match the thinned ones, and beyond that age unmanaged stands accumulate richness faster. The interaction encodes the trade-off between early-successional structure (favored by thinning) and the structural complexity that accumulates in undisturbed mature forest."

Problem 7 — Pileated woodpecker offsets

(a) Analyst B's intercept is the per-minute log-rate at forest_age = 0. Analyst A's intercept is a total-count mean at forest_age = 0 — confounded with however long counts happened to last at age-zero stations.

(b) The offset goes on the log scale because the linear predictor is on the log scale. We want $\log(\mu) = \log(\text{minutes}) + \beta_0 + \beta_1 x$, equivalently $\mu / \text{minutes} = \exp(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x)$: a per-minute rate. Putting effort on the raw scale would not produce this clean rate interpretation.

(c) Say it as biology: "Each decade of forest age was associated with a 4% increase in the per-minute pileated detection rate ($e^{0.04 \cdot 1} = 1.041$, or $e^{0.4} = 1.49$ per 10-decade century of stand development)."

(d) Without an offset, count is a function of (per-minute rate) × (minutes). If long counts cluster in old growth, Analyst A's forest_age coefficient absorbs both biology and effort — inflating the apparent old-growth effect. The offset disentangles them.

m <- glm(detections ~ forest_age + offset(log(minutes)),
 family = poisson, data = pc)
summary(m)
# the forest_age slope is now a per-minute rate effect

Problem 9 — Cone production back-transform with CI

(a) $\exp(0.55) = 1.733$. Dominant trees produce 73% more cones than codominant trees on average.

(b) CI on multiplicative scale: $(\exp(0.32), \exp(0.78)) = (1.377, 2.181)$. Asymmetric on the multiplicative scale — the upper arm is longer in absolute terms (since exp is convex).

(c) Exponentiating the midpoint produces $\exp((0.32 + 0.78)/2) = \exp(0.55) = 1.73$ — by chance the same as the point estimate here because the CI was constructed symmetrically on the log scale (Wald). But this only works because Wald is symmetric in log-space; in general the correct procedure is to back-transform each endpoint. If the CI had been built with a non-symmetric method (likelihood, bootstrap), exponentiating the midpoint would be biased.

exp(0.55) # 1.733
exp(c(0.32, 0.78)) # 1.377 2.181
# Asymmetry:
1.733 - 1.377 # 0.356 (lower arm)
2.181 - 1.733 # 0.448 (upper arm)

(d) Say it as biology: "Dominant trees produced 1.73× the cones of codominant trees (95% CI 1.38× to 2.18×), confirming the well-documented crown-class control of reproductive output in shade-tolerant conifers."

Problem 10 — Hermit thrush threshold

(a) At shrub = 25%: $-3.0 + 0.08 \cdot \text{BA} + 0.04 \cdot 25 = 0 \Rightarrow \text{BA} = 25$ m²/ha.

(b) At shrub = 50%: $-3.0 + 0.08 \cdot \text{BA} + 0.04 \cdot 50 = 0 \Rightarrow \text{BA} = 12.5$ m²/ha.

(c) At $p = 0.5$, marginal effect of BA = $0.08 \cdot 0.5 \cdot 0.5 = 0.020$ per m²/ha — about 2 percentage points of occupancy per m²/ha.

(d) At $p = 0.3$, marginal effects per unit of x:

  • basal area: $0.08 \cdot 0.3 \cdot 0.7 = 0.0168$ per m²/ha → 5 m²/ha gives $\approx +0.084$ probability.
  • shrub: $0.04 \cdot 0.3 \cdot 0.7 = 0.0084$ per % → 10% gives $\approx +0.084$ probability.
Both interventions yield about the same predicted probability gain — but the basal-area gain comes from twice the per-unit coefficient on half the unit change.

# exact computation at p = 0.3
plogis(qlogis(0.3) + 0.08 * 5) # ~ 0.39
plogis(qlogis(0.3) + 0.04 * 10) # ~ 0.39

Say it as biology: "Hermit thrush occupancy thresholds are joint functions of basal area and shrub cover: doubling shrub cover from 25% to 50% halves the basal area required to reach 50% occupancy. From a management standpoint, raising basal area by 5 m²/ha and raising shrub cover by 10 points produce equivalent marginal occupancy gains at a typical low-suitability site — so the choice should be made on cost, not on ecological impact."
