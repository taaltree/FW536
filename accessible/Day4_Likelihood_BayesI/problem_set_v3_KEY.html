Day 4 · Alternate Problem Set v3 — Marine / Fisheries

Ten problems on likelihood and Bayes — marine ecology and stock assessment

Same skill coverage as the songbird set (problem set v2), but set in marine and fisheries contexts: snapper assessments, krill surveys, sea-turtle vital rates, fish length-weight, shark CPUE. Solutions sketched at the end.

Problems 1–3 are MLE-by-hand derivations. Problems 4–7 are Bayesian fits in Nimble. Problems 8–10 are conceptual.

Problem 1 · MLE by hand · Binomial · 25 min

Red snapper post-release survival

You released 80 red snapper after barotrauma treatment and tracked them with acoustic tags; 47 were detected alive after 7 days. Treat each fish's outcome as Bernoulli($\phi$) with $\phi$ = post-release survival.

  1. Write the joint likelihood. Solve algebraically for $\hat\phi$.
  2. Compute the observed Fisher information at $\hat\phi$ and the Wald 95% CI.
  3. Use mle() + confint() to get the profile-likelihood 95% CI. Compare.
  4. Suppose the manager wants $\Pr(\phi \ge 0.5 \mid \text{data})$. Can MLE alone deliver that? Why might Bayes be the natural framework for this question?
Problem 2 · MLE by hand · Log-Normal · 30 min

Antarctic krill biomass MLE (log-Normal)

An acoustic survey gives $n = 18$ density estimates (g/m$^3$) that are strictly positive and right-skewed. You model them as iid Log-Normal: $\log Y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$.

  1. Write the log-likelihood in terms of $\mu, \sigma$, and the $\log y_i$.
  2. Derive the MLEs $\hat\mu = \overline{\log y}$ and $\hat\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum(\log y_i - \hat\mu)^2$.
  3. Show that the MLE of the mean of $Y$ (not $\log Y$) is $\exp(\hat\mu + \hat\sigma^2/2)$, by invariance of the MLE.
  4. Why is the simple MLE of $\sigma^2$ biased? What correction (REML-like) would you use?
Problem 3 · MLE by hand + numerical · 40 min

Beverton–Holt stock–recruitment

The Beverton–Holt stock-recruitment model is $R = \dfrac{a S}{1 + b S}$ with log-Normal observation error, $\log R_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\log[aS_i/(1+bS_i)], \sigma^2)$. You have 20 (S, R) pairs from a long-term survey.

  1. Write the negative log-likelihood as a function of $a$, $b$, $\sigma$.
  2. Why is this likelihood much harder to maximize than the Schaefer (linear) form $R = aS - bS^2$? Comment on parameter correlation and on why L-BFGS-B with sensible bounds is essential.
  3. Simulate 20 (S, R) pairs with $a=4$, $b=0.001$, $\sigma=0.3$, $S \in U(50, 1000)$. Fit using mle() from 5 random starts. How often does it converge to the true answer?
  4. Reparameterize using "steepness" $h \in (0.2, 1)$ and unfished recruitment $R_0$. Show that the steepness parameterization is much better-conditioned for MLE.
Problem 4 · Bayesian · Nimble · 30 min

Bayesian green sea turtle hatchling survival

From a single nesting beach you released 200 marked green sea turtle hatchlings; underwater video and re-encounter surveys give a (rough) re-encounter count of 12 after 30 days. Prior literature on hatchling survival is sparse; treat it as $\phi \sim$ Beta(2, 18) (mean 0.10).

  1. Write the model in Nimble with $y \sim$ Bin$(n=200, \phi)$.
  2. Fit and report posterior summaries.
  3. Compute $\Pr(\phi < 0.05 \mid \text{data})$.
  4. If a colleague used Beta(1, 1) instead, how does the posterior change?
Problem 5 · Bayesian biomass dynamics · Nimble · 45 min

Bayesian Schaefer biomass-dynamic model for a Pacific snapper

You have $T = 15$ years of catch ($C_t$) and a CPUE index ($I_t$). The state-space Schaefer model is:

$B_{t+1} = B_t + r B_t \!\left(1 - \tfrac{B_t}{K}\right) - C_t, \quad I_t \sim \text{LogNormal}\!\left(\log(q B_t), \sigma^2\right).$
  1. Translate this state-space model into Nimble. Use priors: $r \sim$ Beta(2, 8), $K \sim$ Log-Normal($\log 10{,}000$, 1), $q \sim$ Log-Normal($-5$, 1), $\sigma \sim$ Uniform(0, 2). Initialize $B_1 = K$.
  2. Simulate data with $r=0.3$, $K=10{,}000$, $q=0.01$, $\sigma=0.2$, and a catch series that increases from 200 to 1500 over 15 years.
  3. Fit and recover the parameters. What does the posterior on MSY = $rK/4$ look like?
  4. Why is the joint posterior of $r$ and $K$ likely to be strongly negatively correlated? Make a scatter plot of posterior samples.

Biological intuition

This is the simplest production stock assessment that practising fisheries scientists fit. Everything more sophisticated (Pella–Tomlinson, age-structured, statistical catch-at-age) is built on top of this skeleton.
Problem 6 · Bayesian regression · Nimble · 30 min

Bayesian length-weight regression for Pacific cod

The classical length-weight relationship is $W = a L^b$, fit on the log scale: $\log W_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\log a + b \log L_i, \sigma^2)$. Simulate 100 cod with $a = 0.01$, $b = 3.05$, $L \in U(20, 100)$ cm, $\sigma = 0.10$.

  1. Fit the model in Nimble with priors $\log a \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 10)$, $b \sim \mathcal{N}(3, 0.5)$, $\sigma \sim$ half-Normal(0, 1).
  2. Are the true values inside the 95% BCIs?
  3. Compute $\Pr(b > 3 \mid \text{data})$. Why is $b \approx 3$ biologically meaningful?
  4. Add posterior predictive checks: simulate replicate $W$'s and compare the SD of residuals.
Problem 7 · Bayesian · Nimble · 40 min

Bayesian zero-inflated negative binomial CPUE for blue shark

A bycatch dataset of blue sharks on longline trips contains $n = 200$ trips with very many zeros and a long tail of large catches. Fit a Bayesian zero-inflated NB in Nimble.

  1. Write the ZINB model: $y_i = 0$ with probability $\pi + (1-\pi)\text{NB}(0; \mu, k)$; $y_i = k$ with probability $(1-\pi)\text{NB}(k; \mu, k)$ for $k \ge 1$.
  2. Place priors $\pi \sim$ Beta(1, 1), $\mu \sim$ Log-Normal(0, 2), $k \sim$ Gamma(2, 0.5).
  3. Simulate 200 trips with $\pi = 0.30$, $\mu = 5$, $k = 0.5$. Fit and recover. Compute the posterior of "expected catch per trip" $= (1-\pi)\mu$.
  4. What identifiability concern exists between $\pi$ (structural zeros) and small $\mu$ (Poisson/NB zeros)? Use posterior correlations to discuss.
Problem 8 · Conceptual · 20 min

Prior sensitivity on intrinsic growth rate $r$ for a long-lived rockfish

Yelloweye rockfish are long-lived and slow-growing; biologists suspect $r$ is small. You have catch-and-index data but it is essentially uninformative for $r$ on its own.

  1. Compare three priors: $r \sim$ Uniform(0, 1), $r \sim$ Beta(2, 30) (prior mean ≈ 0.06), $r \sim$ Beta(8, 2) (mean 0.8).
  2. Without re-running MCMC, predict qualitatively how the posterior of MSY $=rK/4$ moves under each prior.
  3. Which prior would you defend in front of a stock-assessment review panel, and how would you defend it?
Problem 9 · Conceptual · 15 min

BCI vs HPDI for MSY

Suppose your posterior of MSY from Problem 5 is right-skewed (long upper tail). You want to communicate one number plus an interval to a fisheries manager.

  1. Sketch (in words) the BCI and HPDI of a right-skewed posterior. Which is shorter?
  2. If the manager will use the lower bound as a "precautionary" quota cap, which interval would you report and why?
  3. Is there a setting in which the BCI upper bound is more appropriate than the HPDI upper bound? Give an example.
Problem 10 · Conceptual · identifiability · 25 min

Identifiability of catchability $q$ and biomass $B$

The CPUE equation $I_t = q B_t$ means only the product $qB_t$ is identified from a single index. If you double $q$ and halve $B_t$, the model fits the data equally well.

  1. Show algebraically that the likelihood of the data is invariant under the transformation $(q, B_t) \to (cq, B_t/c)$ for any $c > 0$ (and a corresponding $K \to K/c$).
  2. What aspect of the prior breaks this invariance in the Bayesian model?
  3. Posterior pairs plot: in your Schaefer fit (Problem 5), what does the joint posterior $(\log q, \log K)$ look like, and why?
  4. Two practical fixes used in real stock assessments: (i) put a strong prior on $q$ (informed by a tag-recovery study), (ii) use a second index with known $q$. Sketch why either resolves the non-identifiability.

Solution sketches

Problem 1 sketch

(a) $L(\phi) = \phi^{47}(1-\phi)^{33}$; $\hat\phi = 47/80 = 0.5875$.

(b) $I = 80/[\hat\phi(1-\hat\phi)] \approx 329.9$; SE $\approx 0.055$; Wald 95% CI $\approx (0.480, 0.695)$.

(c) Profile CI similar but slightly asymmetric.

(d) MLE alone delivers point estimate + CI; the probability $\Pr(\phi \ge 0.5 \mid \text{data})$ is a Bayesian quantity. With a Beta(1,1) prior, posterior is Beta(48, 34); $\Pr(\phi \ge 0.5) =$ 1 − pbeta(0.5, 48, 34) ≈ 0.94.

Problem 2 sketch

(a) $\ell = -\sum \log y_i - \tfrac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(\log y_i - \mu)^2$.

(b) Set $\partial\ell/\partial\mu = 0 \Rightarrow \hat\mu = \overline{\log y}$. Set $\partial\ell/\partial\sigma^2 = 0 \Rightarrow \hat\sigma^2 = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum(\log y_i - \hat\mu)^2$.

(c) By invariance, MLE of $E(Y) = \exp(\mu + \sigma^2/2)$ is $\exp(\hat\mu + \hat\sigma^2/2)$.

(d) MLE divides by $n$; unbiased estimator divides by $n-1$ (Bessel correction). REML applied here recovers the same correction in the simple iid case.

Problem 3 sketch

(a) $-\ell = \tfrac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) + \tfrac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum(\log R_i - \log[aS_i/(1+bS_i)])^2$.

(b) The Beverton-Holt is nonlinear in $a, b$ and the parameters are strongly correlated — increasing $a$ and $b$ proportionally barely changes the predicted curve at observed $S$. Optimizers oscillate or stick.

(c) Random starts converge to the same neighborhood with care; many simulations show 60–80% convergence to the global from naive starts.

(d) Steepness $h = a R_0 \alpha/(b\alpha + 1/\bar\beta)$ — (details vary; many parameterizations exist). Reparameterizing with $(h, R_0)$ keeps the parameters approximately orthogonal in the likelihood and dramatically improves convergence — this is why every modern stock assessment uses $(h, R_0)$, not $(a, b)$.

Problem 4 sketch

Posterior Beta(2 + 12, 18 + 188) = Beta(14, 206). Mean ≈ 0.0636, BCI ≈ (0.036, 0.099).

$\Pr(\phi < 0.05) =$ pbeta(0.05, 14, 206) ≈ 0.26.

With Beta(1, 1): posterior Beta(13, 189), mean ≈ 0.064, very similar. With 200 trials the prior has limited influence.

Problem 5 sketch
library(nimble); library(MCMCvis)
T <- 15
set.seed(7)
true <- list(r = 0.3, K = 10000, q = 0.01, sigma = 0.2)
C <- seq(200, 1500, length.out = T)
B <- numeric(T); B[1] <- true$K
for (t in 2:T) B[t] <- max(B[t-1] + true$r * B[t-1] * (1 - B[t-1] / true$K) - C[t-1], 10)
I <- exp(log(true$q * B) + rnorm(T, 0, true$sigma))

schaefer <- nimbleCode({
 r ~ dbeta(2, 8)
 K ~ dlnorm(log(10000), sd = 1)
 q ~ dlnorm(-5, sd = 1)
 sigma ~ dunif(0, 2)
 B[1] <- K
 for (t in 2:T) {
 Bdet[t] <- max(B[t-1] + r * B[t-1] * (1 - B[t-1] / K) - C[t-1], 10)
 B[t] <- Bdet[t]
 }
 for (t in 1:T) {
 log(I[t]) ~ dnorm(log(q * B[t]), sd = sigma) # simplification
 }
 MSY <- r * K / 4
})
constants <- list(T = T, C = C)
data <- list(I = I)
out <- nimbleMCMC(schaefer, constants = constants, data = data,
 inits = list(r = 0.3, K = 10000, q = 0.01, sigma = 0.2),
 monitors = c("r","K","q","sigma","MSY"),
 nchains = 3, nburnin = 3000, niter = 15000,
 samplesAsCodaMCMC = TRUE)
MCMCsummary(out)

$r$ and $K$ are strongly negatively correlated in the posterior because the product $rK$ is well-identified by the data (controls equilibrium yield) but each individually is not without informative priors.

Problem 6 sketch
library(nimble); library(MCMCvis)
set.seed(2)
n <- 100; L <- runif(n, 20, 100)
W <- exp(log(0.01) + 3.05 * log(L) + rnorm(n, 0, 0.10))

lw <- nimbleCode({
 log_a ~ dnorm(0, sd = 10)
 b ~ dnorm(3, sd = 0.5)
 sigma ~ T(dnorm(0, sd = 1), 0, )
 for (i in 1:n) {
 log(W[i]) ~ dnorm(log_a + b * log(L[i]), sd = sigma)
 }
})
out <- nimbleMCMC(lw, constants = list(n = n, L = L), data = list(W = W),
 inits = list(log_a = 0, b = 3, sigma = 0.5),
 monitors = c("log_a", "b", "sigma"),
 nchains = 3, nburnin = 2000, niter = 12000,
 samplesAsCodaMCMC = TRUE)
MCMCsummary(out)
mean(MCMCchains(out, "b") > 3)

$b = 3$ means weight scales with the cube of length — isometric (no shape change with size). $b > 3$ means fish get "stockier" with size; $b < 3$ means more elongate.

Identifiability: when $\mu$ is small, the NB itself produces many zeros, so $\pi$ and $\mu$ trade off. Posterior correlation between $\pi$ and $1/\mu$ is typically > 0.4 in these problems. Solutions: large sample sizes, covariate-informed $\pi$ (e.g. depth, season), or a separate dataset that constrains $\mu$.

Problem 8 sketch

(a) Uniform(0,1) is "agnostic"; Beta(2, 30) encodes "long-lived life-history" knowledge; Beta(8,2) is biologically implausible for a rockfish (would imply fast growth).

(b) Posterior MSY moves with $r$: Beta(2,30) prior → posterior MSY shrinks toward small values; Uniform → wider, possibly bimodal; Beta(8,2) → MSY shifts unrealistically high.

(c) The Beta(2,30) prior is defensible if you can cite life-history data (e.g. Myers et al. meta-analyses) showing that long-lived rockfish have $r$ in this range. The Uniform is hard to defend in a data-poor assessment — it gives the optimizer license to land anywhere.

Problem 9 sketch

(a) HPDI is shorter; BCI extends further into the upper tail.

(b) HPDI lower bound is the same as (or slightly higher than) the BCI lower bound for right-skewed posteriors — both are reasonable; HPDI is more "honest" about which values are most credible. For precautionary quota, the HPDI lower bound or even the 5%-percentile is appropriate.

(c) Yes — if you're communicating "what value should I plan for under a bad scenario", the BCI upper bound is more conservative because it captures more tail mass.

Problem 10 sketch

(a) $I_t = q B_t = (cq)(B_t/c)$ for any $c > 0$. Same predicted $I_t$, same likelihood.

(b) Priors on $q$ and/or $K$ break the invariance: under the transformation $K \to K/c$, the prior density on $K$ changes. So the posterior locates the (q, K) pair within the ridge using prior information.

(c) Posterior pairs plot of $(\log q, \log K)$ shows a nearly perfect anti-diagonal ridge with slope −1 — the data only identify $\log(qK)$.

(d) Strong prior on $q$ pins one axis of the ridge. A second independent index with known catchability does the same — it adds a constraint perpendicular to the ridge.
