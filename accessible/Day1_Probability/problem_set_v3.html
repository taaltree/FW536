Problem 1 · Bernoulli/Binomial

Lynx tracks on a snow transect

A 5 km transect is divided into 50 segments of 100 m each. At a given snow depth and time of year, the probability of detecting at least one lynx track in any given segment is $p = 0.06$, independently.

  1. Expected number of segments with detections? Variance?
  2. What is the probability of detecting tracks in ≥ 5 segments?
  3. What is the probability the whole transect has zero detections?
Problem 2 · Bayes' rule

Avian influenza screening

A flock of waterfowl has prior infection prevalence 0.08. A swab test has sensitivity 0.95 and specificity 0.90.

  1. Compute the posterior probability a swab-positive bird is infected.
  2. Suppose three independent swabs from the same bird all come back positive. What is the posterior?
  3. If two come back positive and one negative, what is the posterior? (Assume conditional independence.)
Problem 3 · Poisson

Wolf encounters at active dens

An observer records wolf approaches to a den at an average rate of $\lambda = 1.8$ per 12-h observation period.

  1. Write the model. What does it assume about independence of approaches?
  2. P(no approaches in a 12-h period)?
  3. P(more than 3 approaches in a 12-h period)?
  4. P(exactly 5 approaches in a 36-h period)?
Problem 4 · Beta-binomial preview

Bobcat camera grid heterogeneity

Eight remote cameras are deployed for one week. Mean per-camera detection probability is $\bar p = 0.20$, but cameras vary widely in placement quality.

  1. Under a plain Binomial(8, 0.20), what is P(0 cameras detect)?
  2. Suppose camera detection probabilities are drawn from Beta(1.6, 6.4) (mean 0.2, much variability). Simulate per-camera $p_i$, then per-camera Bernoulli outcomes. Repeat 10,000 times. What is the simulated P(0 detect)?
  3. Why does heterogeneity inflate the probability of "all zeros"?
Problem 5 · Poisson + zero-inflation

Songbird point counts

At hermit thrush point counts, the average count is 2.4 birds, but ~25% of points are in unsuitable habitat where the true count is 0.

  1. Write the data-generating mixture (suitable / unsuitable habitat).
  2. Simulate 1000 point counts. Plot the histogram.
  3. What is the marginal mean and variance? Compare to a pure Poisson(2.4).
Problem 6 · Multinomial

Owl pellet contents

Barn owl pellet contents from a regional study are composed of voles 0.65, shrews 0.20, mice 0.10, birds 0.05. A pellet contains 15 identifiable prey items.

  1. P(exact composition (10, 3, 1, 1))?
  2. Marginal distribution of "shrews" in a pellet?
  3. Across 50 pellets, expected total of mice consumed?
Problem 7 · Negative binomial

Coyote scat counts on grid cells

Coyote scats per grid cell from a recent survey have mean 4 and variance 28.

  1. What dispersion $k$ matches these moments?
  2. What is P(0)? P(>15)?
  3. What ecological mechanisms might produce the over-dispersion?
Problem 8 · Surveillance design

Brucellosis in Yellowstone bison

Hypothetical herd prevalence is 0.15. You sample $n$ bison. You want at least 95% probability of detecting at least one positive bison.

  1. Write the binomial probability of detecting at least one positive.
  2. Solve algebraically (or numerically in R) for the smallest $n$ that delivers 95% detection probability.
  3. Repeat for prevalence = 0.02.
Problem 9 · Expected value via indicators

Solitary cougars in a linear gradient

$n=30$ cougar territories are arranged linearly along a ridgeline. Each cougar independently has probability $p=0.3$ of "calling tonight." Count the expected number of isolated callers — a cougar that calls, but neither immediate neighbor calls. Consider only the 28 interior cougars.

  1. Compute $E[X]$ using indicator variables.
  2. Verify by simulation (20,000 reps).
Problem 10 · Joint probability + composite hypothesis

Grizzly bear DNA from hair snares

A hair snare grid has 40 stations. The probability of getting at least one usable hair sample at a station in one occasion is $p = 0.18$ when a bear is present. Bears are present at 60% of stations.

  1. Probability of "at least one sample" at a randomly chosen station? (Hint: condition on presence.)
  2. Given a station gets ≥ 1 sample, what is the probability a bear was present there?
  3. If you visit every station 3 times, what is the per-station probability of at least one sample across the three visits (assume independence between visits | presence)?
