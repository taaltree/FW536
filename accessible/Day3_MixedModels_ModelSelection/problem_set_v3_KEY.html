Aquatic and fisheries scenarios — mixed models and model selection

Part 1 · Mixed models

Problem 1 · pooling decision

Chinook salmon redds nested in streams

A redd survey covers 15 streams in the Willamette basin; each stream is walked once and the number of Chinook redds per 100 m of stream is recorded for 4 segments per stream. The science question: are mean redd densities meaningfully different among streams, and can we use the random structure to predict for new streams in the basin?

  1. Write the complete pooling, no pooling, and partial pooling models in math.
  2. The lead biologist proposes treating "stream" as fixed effects so each stream's mean is explicitly estimated. What is gained and what is lost relative to a random-effect treatment?
  3. If $\sigma_\text{stream} \approx 4$ redds/100m and $\sigma_\text{residual} \approx 1.5$ redds/100m, what is the ICC? What does the ICC say about how much partial pooling will shrink any one stream's estimate?
Answer

(a) Let $y_{ij}$ be the redd density in segment $i$ of stream $j$.

  • Complete pooling: $y_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta_0, \sigma^2)$.
  • No pooling: $y_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha_j, \sigma^2)$, $j = 1,\ldots,15$.
  • Partial pooling: $y_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(\beta_0 + b_{0,j}, \sigma^2)$, $b_{0,j} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_S^2)$.

(b) Gained with fixed effects: explicit, unshrunk estimate of each stream's mean and its CI; no assumption about a "population of streams." Lost: 15 extra parameters to estimate; no way to predict for a stream not in the dataset; less efficient estimates for streams with few segments.

(c) ICC = $16/(16 + 2.25) = 0.877$. About 88% of variation is between streams. With only 4 segments per stream, partial-pool shrinkage is small but real — extreme streams move a few percent toward the grand mean.

Problem 2 · two crossed random effects

Stream temperature loggers across watersheds and years

You deploy temperature loggers in 6 watersheds, 5 sites per watershed, sampled in 3 years. The response is summer mean temperature. You want to test whether shading (canopy cover %) predicts temperature, after accounting for watershed and year.

m <- lmer(SummerTemp ~ scale(Canopy) + (1 | Watershed) + (1 | Year), data=d)
summary(m)
# Random effects:
# Groups Name Variance Std.Dev.
# Year (Intercept) 0.71 0.84
# Watershed (Intercept) 1.94 1.39
# Residual 0.46 0.68
# Fixed effects:
# Estimate Std.Error t
# (Intercept) 14.20 0.66 21.5
# scale(Canopy) -1.30 0.18 -7.2
  1. Why are Watershed and Year specified as crossed (with +) rather than nested (with /)?
  2. What fraction of variation is between watersheds, between years, and within (residual)?
  3. Interpret the canopy slope in real units. Hint: a 1-SD change in canopy on the original scale produces a 1.3°C change in expected summer temperature.
  4. If the same site IDs (1, 2, 3, 4, 5) are reused across watersheds, what happens if you write (1 | Site) instead of (1 | Watershed:Site)?
Answer

(a) Years are not nested within watersheds — every watershed was sampled in every year. The two grouping variables are crossed, so we use +. Nested syntax (/) would be appropriate if e.g. each year had its own unique set of watersheds.

(b) Total = $0.71 + 1.94 + 0.46 = 3.11$. Watershed: 62%. Year: 23%. Residual: 15%. Most variation is across watersheds (consistent with different elevations, baseflows, riparian conditions).

(c) A 1-SD increase in canopy cover (perhaps ~15% absolute) lowers expected summer mean temperature by 1.3°C, with SE 0.18 → 95% CI ≈ (-1.66, -0.95). Strong cooling effect of canopy.

(d) If Site IDs are reused, (1 | Site) treats e.g. "Site 1" in Watershed A and "Site 1" in Watershed B as the same site (sharing one random effect). That is biologically wrong. Use (1 | Watershed:Site) or, equivalently, add a unique Site_id column.

Problem 3 · nonlinear mixed (vonB)

Steelhead growth: individual-level $L_\infty$

You have repeated length-at-age data for 25 PIT-tagged steelhead, each measured 4–10 times over 5 years. Fit a fixed-effect von Bertalanffy via nls and a mixed-effect version via nlme with a random $L_\infty$ per fish.

library(nlme)
m_nls <- nls(Length ~ Linf*(1 - exp(-K*(Age - t0))),
 data=steel, start=c(Linf=80, K=0.25, t0=-0.5))
m_nlme <- nlme(Length ~ Linf*(1 - exp(-K*(Age - t0))),
 fixed = Linf + K + t0 ~ 1,
 random = Linf ~ 1 | FishID,
 data = steel, start = c(Linf=80, K=0.25, t0=-0.5))
summary(m_nls)$sigma # e.g., 6.2
summary(m_nlme)$sigma # e.g., 2.5
VarCorr(m_nlme) # SD(Linf | FishID) ≈ 5.8
  1. How much variation in growth is among individuals (between-fish) vs within an individual (measurement / time)?
  2. What does the BLUP for one fish's $L_\infty$ look like, and how would you compute it from m_nlme$coefficients?
  3. Why is the within-fish residual SE from nlme systematically smaller than the residual SE from the fixed-effects nls?
Answer

(a) Between-fish: $5.8^2 = 33.6$ (cm$^2$ of $L_\infty$ variance). Within: $2.5^2 = 6.25$. Most variation in length-at-age across individuals is the asymptotic body size differing among fish.

(b) m_nlme$coefficients$fixed["Linf"] + m_nlme$coefficients$random$FishID[ , "Linf"] gives the per-fish posterior mean (BLUP). Equivalent to coef(m_nlme)[, "Linf"].

(c) The mixed model carves out among-individual variation into its own variance component. What used to count as residual in nls ($\sigma^2_\text{total}$) is now partitioned: $\sigma^2_\text{nls} \approx \sigma^2_{L_\infty} + \sigma^2_\text{within}$. So $\sigma_\text{within} < \sigma_\text{nls}$ by construction.

Problem 4 · binomial GLMM

Sockeye smolt detection at PIT arrays

20,000 PIT-tagged sockeye smolts pass through 8 mainstem detection arrays. Each tag is observed at each array (1) or not (0). You fit:

library(lme4)
m <- glmer(Detect ~ scale(Flow) + scale(Turbidity) + (1 | TagID) + (1 | Array),
 data=d, family=binomial)
VarCorr(m)
# Groups Name Variance Std.Dev.
# TagID (Intercept) 0.62 0.79
# Array (Intercept) 2.10 1.45
# Fixed effects:
# Estimate Std.Error z
# (Intercept) -0.85 0.55 -1.5
# scale(Flow) -0.22 0.04 -5.5
# scale(Turbidity) -0.41 0.04 -10.3
  1. Why include TagID as a random intercept here?
  2. Translate the array-level SD into a "typical odds-ratio range" across arrays (how much do detection odds vary between a strong array and a weak array, ± 2 SD apart)?
  3. Interpret the Turbidity slope as an odds ratio with 95% CI.
  4. For a typical array and typical tag, what is the detection probability at average flow and turbidity?
Answer

(a) Detection events for the same tag at multiple arrays are not independent — some tags are easier to detect (good battery, well-positioned). The tag random intercept absorbs that individual heterogeneity, leaving Array and covariates to explain among-detection-occasion variation honestly.

(b) ± 2 SD on the logit scale = $\pm 2 \times 1.45 = \pm 2.9$. So a strong array vs a weak array differs by a factor of $e^{5.8} \approx 330$ in detection odds — substantial.

(c) Odds ratio per 1 SD turbidity = $e^{-0.41} = 0.66$. 95% CI: $e^{-0.41 \pm 1.96 \times 0.04} = (0.61, 0.72)$. Each 1-SD increase in turbidity multiplies detection odds by 0.66 — a strong negative effect.

(d) $\text{plogis}(-0.85) \approx 0.30$.

Problem 5 · random slopes vs random intercepts

Pacific lamprey emergence × temperature, across rivers

You have 8 rivers and 5 years of lamprey emergence counts vs spring water temperature. You fit:

m_int <- lmer(log_emerg ~ scale(Temp) + (1 | River), REML=TRUE, data=d)
m_slope <- lmer(log_emerg ~ scale(Temp) + (1 + scale(Temp) | River), REML=TRUE, data=d)
anova(m_int, m_slope, refit=FALSE)
# Df AIC logLik Chisq Chi Df Pr(>Chisq)
# m_int 4
# m_slope 6 9.4 2 0.009
  1. Does the LRT support the random slope?
  2. The boundary-test caveat applies — adjust the p-value accordingly.
  3. If VarCorr(m_slope) shows the slope-intercept correlation = +0.99, what is happening, and what is one fix?
  4. Compare the population-average slope (fixed effect) between m_int and m_slope. If they differ noticeably, which would you report?
Answer

(a) Yes. $\chi^2 = 9.4$ on 2 df, $p = 0.009$ — strong evidence that the temperature slope varies by river.

(b) Testing one variance component on the boundary uses a 50:50 mixture $\chi^2$ null; the standard test is conservative. The corrected p-value is roughly half — even more support.

(c) A correlation of +0.99 indicates a singular fit: the (intercept, slope) covariance matrix is on the boundary of the positive-definite cone. Fix: drop the correlation with (1 | River) + (0 + scale(Temp) | River), or simplify the random structure to intercept-only.

(d) Report the random-slope model's fixed-effect slope if it's supported by the LRT — its CI accounts for among-river variability in the slope. The intercept-only model's CI on the slope is too narrow because it treats the slope as fixed across rivers.

Part 2 · Model selection

Problem 6 · LRT and the boundary issue

Bull trout occupancy: testing a random effect with LRT

You fit two GLMMs of bull trout occupancy in 120 streams:

m_no <- glm (Occ ~ scale(Elev) + scale(Width), family=binomial, data=d)
m_with <- glmer(Occ ~ scale(Elev) + scale(Width) + (1 | Basin), family=binomial, data=d)
anova(m_no, m_with, test="LRT")
# Df Deviance Chisq Chi Df Pr(>Chisq)
# 2.2 1 0.138
  1. The LRT compares "no random effect" to "with random effect." What is the boundary issue and how should we correct the p-value?
  2. After correction, would you retain the basin random effect?
  3. What is the practical risk of dropping a random effect that the LRT calls "non-significant"?
  4. What would you do if you only had 4 basins instead of 30?
Answer

(a) Testing whether $\sigma_\text{Basin}^2 = 0$ puts the parameter on the boundary. The correct asymptotic null is $\tfrac{1}{2}\chi^2_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}\chi^2_1$. Divide the reported p-value by 2: $p = 0.069$.

(b) Borderline. Many practitioners would retain the random effect because it costs only 1 parameter, the LRT is conservative on the boundary, and dropping it gives you anti-conservative inference on the fixed effects if there is real basin clustering.

(c) Dropping a random effect that "shouldn't have been there" doesn't bias fixed-effect estimates much, but does over-tighten their CIs because the residual variance is now smaller. The pragmatic rule: if observations are clustered by basin in design (and they are, here), keep the random effect even if its variance is small.

(d) With only 4 basins, the variance component is essentially unidentifiable. Use a fixed effect for basin, or report results separately for each basin if that makes more scientific sense.

Problem 7 · dredge danger

Dungeness crab pot CPUE — full-subsets selection on 10 predictors

You have 240 pot soaks with CPUE (count per pot per soak) and 10 candidate predictors: depth, substrate, distance-to-shore, kelp density, soak duration, bait type (5-level factor), and a few interactions. A colleague proposes running dredge() on a Poisson GLM and reporting the "best" model.

  1. How many candidate models does dredge() consider?
  2. What is the family-wise Type I error rate of "at least one noise variable in the top model" likely to be?
  3. Outline a defensible alternative workflow.
  4. If the colleague insists, what one diagnostic should they at least include?
Answer

(a) With 10 marginal predictors and (some) interactions, on the order of $2^{12}$ or more — thousands of candidate models. dredge() can take a long time and the multiplicity is severe.

(b) Simulations suggest > 90% — under noise alone, dredging this many candidates almost always retains at least one noise variable in the top AICc model.

(c) Defensible workflow: (i) build a small set (3–6) of biologically motivated candidate models before looking at the data; (ii) compare with AICc and report the variance components and CIs of fixed effects from each; (iii) validate the chosen model on a held-out subset of pots (or via $k$-fold CV); (iv) if all-subsets selection really is needed, use a regularized regression (LASSO/elastic-net) that explicitly handles the multiplicity.

(d) $k$-fold cross-validation of the selected "best" model — directly measures out-of-sample prediction error and reveals overfitting that AICc does not.

Problem 8 · BIC vs AIC trade-off

Coastal cutthroat smolt-to-adult return rate

You want to test 3 predictors of SAR: ocean_temp_anomaly (strong known driver), juvenile_size (modest), parr_density (weak). $n = 35$ year-stream combinations.

set.seed(11); n <- 35
ot <- rnorm(n); js <- rnorm(n); pd <- rnorm(n)
sar <- 0.6*ot + 0.25*js + 0.06*pd + rnorm(n, 0, 0.4)
d <- data.frame(sar, ot, js, pd)
m <- lm(sar ~ ot + js + pd, data=d)
library(MuMIn); options(na.action="na.fail")
dredge(m, rank="AICc"); dredge(m, rank="BIC")
  1. Run the simulation 500 times. Report the proportion of replicates in which each criterion retains pd (the weak predictor).
  2. Same for js (modest) and ot (strong).
  3. If the science question is "what governs cutthroat SAR?", which criterion would you choose and why?
Answer

(a) Approximate proportions of retaining pd in the top model:

CriterionP(retain pd)
AIC~0.50
AICc~0.45
BIC~0.15

(b) Approximate proportions:

PredictorAICAICcBIC
js (modest)~0.90~0.88~0.70
ot (strong)1.001.001.00

(c) Because the question explicitly allows for weak drivers being important, prefer AICc — it doesn't penalize so heavily that it drops modest signals. If the question were "which predictors are essential for prediction?", BIC's stricter penalty might be defensible. Better still: report the coefficients and CIs from the full a-priori model and let the reader evaluate the effect sizes.

Problem 9 · LOOCV for prediction

Eulachon (smelt) abundance forecasting

You have 18 years of eulachon escapement counts in the Columbia, with annual covariates: spring_outflow, SST, NPGO, predator_index. The agency wants a model to predict next year's run.

library(boot)
forms <- list(
 null = log_run ~ 1,
 flow = log_run ~ spring_outflow,
 fs = log_run ~ spring_outflow + SST,
 fsn = log_run ~ spring_outflow + SST + NPGO,
 fsnp = log_run ~ spring_outflow + SST + NPGO + predator_index)
cv <- sapply(forms, function(f) cv.glm(d, glm(f, data=d))$delta[1])
aic <- sapply(forms, function(f) AICc::AICc(glm(f, data=d)))
data.frame(cv, aic)
  1. With $n = 18$ years and up to 5 parameters, why is AICc essential rather than AIC?
  2. Suppose the smallest LOOCV MSE belongs to fs but the smallest AICc belongs to fsn. How do you resolve the disagreement for the forecasting task?
  3. For a forecasting application, what is one extra validation step beyond LOOCV you should consider?
  4. What is the danger of using ICs of any kind on a tiny dataset like this?
Answer

(a) For the fsnp model, $n/k = 18/5 = 3.6$. AICc's correction $2k(k+1)/(n-k-1) = 2 \times 5 \times 6 / 12 = 5$ — substantial. Without it, AIC would systematically favor over-fitted models.

(b) For a forecasting question, trust LOOCV (it directly measures held-out prediction). AICc disagreeing tells you the asymptotic AICc approximation is breaking down at $n=18$. Report both and the chosen model, and ideally validate on a held-out year if you can spare it.

(c) Time-series-aware CV: predict each year from data before it (one-step-ahead CV), which respects the temporal ordering. Standard LOOCV mixes future and past in the training set.

(d) The variance of the IC differences is enormous when $n$ is small. The "best" model in one realization may not be the best in another with the same generating process. Report uncertainty: bootstrap the data and re-run the selection, counting how often each model is "best."

Problem 10 · multimodel inference + the "right question" trap

Pacific halibut size-at-age and the inference question

A halibut age/length dataset is fit with three competing candidate models: M1 has a fixed temperature effect, M2 has a fixed regional effect, M3 has both. AICc table:

Model K AICc deltaAICc weight
M3 5 1240.0 0.0 0.45
M1 4 1240.6 0.6 0.33
M2 4 1241.9 1.9 0.18
M0 3 1244.7 4.7 0.04
  1. By the "$\Delta\text{AIC} < 2$" rule, which models are competitive?
  2. What is the Akaike-weighted prediction at a specific (age, region, temp) using M1, M2, M3 with weights from the table?
  3. The biologist's actual question is "does temperature affect halibut size-at-age?" Re-frame the model-selection results to answer that question directly (hint: think about evidence ratios, not "the best model").
  4. If the temperature coefficient in M3 is $\hat\beta_T = 0.03$ with SE 0.02, what does that say about the practical importance of temperature, irrespective of which model "wins"?
Answer

(a) M3, M1, M2 — all within 2 AICc units of each other.

(b) If $\hat y_{M1}, \hat y_{M2}, \hat y_{M3}$ are the predictions, the model-averaged prediction (renormalizing weights to sum to 1 over the included set, or using the full weights) is

w <- c(M3=0.45, M1=0.33, M2=0.18, M0=0.04)
pred_avg <- sum(w * c(yhat_M3, yhat_M1, yhat_M2, yhat_M0))

The bulk of the weight is on M3 and M1 — both include temperature — so the averaged prediction will be close to M3's.

(c) Sum the Akaike weights of models that contain temperature (M3 + M1 = 0.78) vs the sum of those that don't (M2 + M0 = 0.22). The "evidence ratio" for temperature is $0.78/0.22 \approx 3.5$ — moderate but not overwhelming evidence that temperature matters. This is more informative than "model M3 wins" because it isolates the inference question.

(d) $\hat\beta_T = 0.03$, SE 0.02 → 95% CI ≈ (-0.01, 0.07). The CI overlaps zero, and the point estimate is small relative to plausible biological scales. Even if M3 "wins" on AICc, the temperature effect is uncertain and likely small. This is the inference message that should make it into the paper — not "we chose M3 with $w = 0.45$."
