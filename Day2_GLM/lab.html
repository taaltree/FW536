Where we are

Yesterday we built probability and met the discrete distributions. This morning we'll meet the continuous distributions you need for regression: Normal, Lognormal, Gamma, Beta, Exponential. This afternoon we'll plug those distributions into a linear-model framework, generalize via link functions, and fit our first GLMs: logistic regression (Bernoulli/Binomial), Poisson regression with log link, and Negative-Binomial regression for overdispersed counts.

The unifying idea today: a GLM is a regression in which the linear predictor $\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \dots$ is connected to a distribution's mean through a link function $g$, so that $g(\mu_i) = \eta_i$. The Normal regression you already know is just a GLM with the identity link. Everything else is a small variation on that template.

Plain-language overview

Regression is just "draw a line (or curve) through the cloud of data, then interpret what the slope means biologically." The catch is that the response variable might be a count, a proportion, or a strictly positive measurement — none of which behave like a Normal. The GLM framework lets you keep the line-drawing logic while choosing a distribution that fits the response.

Two new ideas you'll learn today:

  1. A link function stretches the response to a scale where a straight line makes sense. The log link stretches positive numbers; the logit link stretches probabilities.
  2. Once you fit the model, you have to back-transform coefficients to talk about them biologically. A slope of $+0.5$ on the log scale means a $\times 1.65$ multiplicative effect on the raw scale.

Linear model anatomy

A linear model has three pieces:

PieceWhat it isSymbol
Linear predictorWhat you add up from coefficients and predictors$\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + \ldots$
Link functionHow $\eta$ relates to the mean of the response$g(\mu_i) = \eta_i$
DistributionHow observations vary around the mean$y_i \sim D(\mu_i, \theta)$

The Normal regression you know from intro statistics is the special case $g(\mu) = \mu$ (identity link) and $D = \text{Normal}$:

$y_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \sigma^2), \quad \mu_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i$

Once we relax the link and the distribution, we get the GLM "menu":

Continuous distributions (morning preview)

Normal — the workhorse continuous distribution

Story: sums of many small independent contributions (central limit theorem). Symmetric, no boundary, support $(-\infty, \infty)$.

Parameters: mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$.

Use it when: the response can take any real value, with roughly symmetric scatter.

Caveat: almost no ecological variable is truly unbounded; check residuals after fitting.

Lognormal — when log(Y) is Normal

Story: products of many small independent contributions. Always positive, right-skewed, with multiplicative variation.

Parameters: $\mu, \sigma$ on the log scale; on the raw scale, mean is $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$.

Use it when: the response is positive and right-skewed; the variability scales with the mean.

Ecology: body sizes, abundances at sites, eDNA concentrations.

Gamma — flexible positive continuous

Story: waiting time for $k$ independent Poisson events (sum of exponentials).

Parameters: shape $\alpha$, rate $\beta$. Mean $= \alpha/\beta$; variance $= \alpha/\beta^2$.

Use it when: response is positive and continuous; can be right-skewed but doesn't have to be.

Common GLM choice: glm(y ~ x, family=Gamma(link="log")) for positive responses where variance grows with mean.

Beta — for proportions between 0 and 1

Story: a distribution over probabilities. Two shape parameters $\alpha, \beta$.

Mean $= \alpha/(\alpha+\beta)$. Beta(1,1) is uniform on [0,1].

Use it when: response is a proportion that can take any value strictly between 0 and 1 (not a count out of a fixed total).

Ecology: percent cover, individual-level detection probabilities (the heterogeneity component of beta-binomial).

Exponential — waiting time for one event

Story: time until the first event in a Poisson process at rate $\lambda$. Memoryless.

Mean $= 1/\lambda$.

Ecology: time-between-detections in a camera-trap; line-transect distances to first sighting.

The log link, biologically (this is the big one)

Log-link coefficients describe multiplicative effects, not additive ones. A one-unit change in $x$ multiplies the mean response by $e^\beta$. This single fact, once internalized, makes Poisson/NB regression and lognormal regression both interpretable in a way that "the slope is 0.43" never is.

Worked biological examples

Example 1

Salmon redds along a stream gradient

You fit glm(redds ~ km_upstream, family=poisson(link="log")) and get $\hat\beta_1 = -0.05$. What does this mean biologically?

Interpret

$e^{-0.05} = 0.951$. So for each additional km upstream, expected redd counts fall by ~4.9%. After 14 km, the expected count is $e^{-0.05 \cdot 14} = e^{-0.70} = 0.50$ of the count at the stream mouth — half as many redds.

A useful rule: $-0.05$ per km translates to a half-life of ${\sim}\ln(2)/0.05 = 13.9$ km. This "half-life" language is intuitive for biologists who think about exponential decay.

Example 2

Marine megafauna detections vs. sea state

Cetacean detections per 100 km of transect, modeled as Poisson with predictor Beaufort sea state. $\hat\beta = -0.41$ per unit sea state. What does this mean?

Interpret

$e^{-0.41} = 0.664$. Each Beaufort unit reduces expected detection rate by ~34%. Going from sea state 1 to 4 multiplies the expected count by $0.664^3 = 0.293$, a 71% drop. This is a strong "detection-availability" effect — useful for surveying logistics.

Example 3

Log-link Poisson with a categorical predictor (treatment)

You fit glm(caterpillars ~ treatment, family=poisson) where treatment is a factor with levels control and fertilizer. The fertilizer coefficient is $+0.85$. What does this say?

Interpret

$e^{0.85} = 2.34$. The fertilizer treatment more than doubles the expected caterpillar count compared to control. This is the kind of "fold change" language ecologists already speak in — but they have to remember to exponentiate.

Example 4

Log-link with continuous interaction

You fit glm(catch ~ temp * gear, family=poisson). The main effect of temp is $-0.05$; the temp:gearTrawl interaction is $+0.08$.

  1. What is the temperature effect on the reference gear (gillnet)?
  2. What is the temperature effect on the trawl gear?
Interpret

(a) For gillnet: slope is $-0.05$ per degree, i.e., $e^{-0.05} = 0.951$ → 4.9% drop per degree warming.

(b) For trawl: combined slope is $-0.05 + 0.08 = +0.03$ per degree, i.e., $e^{0.03} = 1.030$ → 3% increase per degree warming.

So the gears respond oppositely to temperature — a finding you can't extract from a single coefficient.

Common mistake. Students often report coefficients on the log scale as if they were proportional changes. Saying "the effect of treatment is 0.85" is true only on the log scale. Always exponentiate when communicating to biologists. exp(coef(fit)) is your friend.

The logit link, biologically

Logit-link coefficients describe odds ratios. A one-unit change in $x$ multiplies the odds by $e^\beta$. Odds aren't probabilities — they're $p/(1-p)$. This is one of the most-confused topics in applied stats, and we'll fix it today.

Three scales, three interpretations

ScaleDefinitionRangeUse to talk to…
Probability $p$chance of success0 to 1biologists, managers, the public
Odds $p/(1-p)$"how many times more likely success is than failure"0 to $\infty$statisticians, gamblers
Log-odds $\log(p/(1-p))$logit; what the linear predictor outputs$-\infty$ to $\infty$the model machinery

The "1.5 rule": coefficient size to probability change

For values of $p$ near 0.5, a log-odds coefficient of $\beta$ translates roughly to a $\beta/4$ change in probability per unit $x$. (E.g., $\beta = 1$ → about a 25-percentage-point increase at the inflection.) Use this rule for back-of-envelope reasoning; use predict(fit, type="response") for actual values.

Example 5

Marten occupancy vs. canopy cover

You fit glm(occupied ~ canopy, family=binomial) with canopy on a 0–100 scale and $\hat\beta_1 = +0.06$ per percent canopy. Interpret.

Interpret

$e^{0.06} = 1.062$. Each additional percentage point of canopy multiplies the odds of occupancy by 1.062. A 10-point increase multiplies odds by $e^{0.6} = 1.82$.

For a site that is currently occupied with $p = 0.3$ (odds = 0.43), a 10-point increase in canopy moves odds to $0.43 \cdot 1.82 = 0.78$, i.e., $p = 0.78 / 1.78 = 0.44$. That's a 14 percentage-point increase — sizable.

Example 6

Survival of fledgling sage grouse — categorical predictor

You fit glm(fledge ~ sex, family=binomial). Coefficient for males vs. females is $-0.4$. Interpret in three ways.

Interpret on three scales
  • Log-odds: males have log-odds 0.4 lower than females.
  • Odds ratio: $e^{-0.4} = 0.67$. Males' odds of fledging are 67% of females' odds.
  • Probability: if females fledge with p = 0.6 (odds = 1.5), males have odds $0.67 \cdot 1.5 = 1.0$, so $p = 0.5$. A 10-percentage-point difference.
Example 7

Nonlinear shape of the logistic curve

Why does a fixed $\beta$ translate to different probability changes depending on where you are on the curve?

Reveal

The logistic function $p(\eta) = e^\eta/(1+e^\eta)$ is steepest at $\eta = 0$ (where $p = 0.5$) and flattens out near $p = 0$ and $p = 1$. So one unit of $x$ moves $p$ a lot in the middle and only a little at the ends. This is biologically realistic — a 10-degree warming moves survival a lot when survival is near 50%, but barely changes it if survival is already 99%.

For a much deeper dive — including marginal-effect computations, the choice between odds ratios and risk ratios, and a worked Titanic example — see the log/logit intuition guide.

Exercise M1 · Exponential

Orangutan line transects

The detection function for orangutans on a line transect is roughly exponential, with rate $\lambda = 0.02$ per meter of perpendicular distance. Derive the survival function (probability of not yet detecting at distance $x$) and use it to compute the probability of missing an orangutan at 50 m.

Reveal

For an Exponential$(\lambda)$, $S(x) = P(X \ge x) = e^{-\lambda x}$. At $x = 50$, $S = e^{-1.0} = 0.368$. So a 50-m-distant orangutan has a 37% chance of being missed.

Exercise M2 · Normal linear transformations

Prove that linear transformations of Normals are Normal

Let $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $Y = \mu + \sigma Z$. Using $E[aZ + b] = a E[Z] + b$ and $\mathrm{Var}(aZ+b) = a^2 \mathrm{Var}(Z)$, show $E[Y] = \mu$ and $\mathrm{Var}(Y) = \sigma^2$.

Reveal

$E[Y] = E[\mu + \sigma Z] = \mu + \sigma \cdot 0 = \mu$. $\mathrm{Var}(Y) = \mathrm{Var}(\mu + \sigma Z) = \sigma^2 \cdot 1 = \sigma^2$. The shape stays Normal because a linear transform of a Gaussian is Gaussian.

Exercise M3 · Beta-Binomial

Marten cameras with heterogeneous detection

200 trail cameras are deployed. Plain assumption: per-camera detection probability $p = 0.03$ each night. But cameras vary: $p_i \sim \text{Beta}(0.03 \cdot \phi, 0.97 \cdot \phi)$ with $\phi = 1$ (so beta is very spread out).

  1. What is the mean of this Beta?
  2. Simulate 5000 deployments under plain Binomial(200, 0.03) and the Beta-Binomial. Compare P(all-zero) and mean/SD of the detection count.
  3. Why does the Beta-Binomial put much more mass on "all-zero"?
Reveal
set.seed(1); reps <- 5000
bin <- rbinom(reps, 200, 0.03)
betbin <- replicate(reps, sum(rbinom(200, 1, rbeta(200, 0.03, 0.97))))
mean(bin==0); mean(betbin==0) # ~ 0 vs ~ 0.04
mean(bin); mean(betbin) # ~ 6 vs ~ 6
sd(bin); sd(betbin) # ~ 2.4 vs ~ 14

(c) In the Beta-Binomial, the detection probability is concentrated near 0 or 1 with this $\phi$, so most cameras get nearly 0 chance, a few get nearly 1. The mass at all-zero is the case where every camera drew a tiny $p_i$.

Afternoon exercises

Exercise A1 · Normal vs Poisson vs NB on eDNA counts

Sockeye eDNA — find the right distribution

Load sockeye_adult.csv (in this Day's data/ folder). The response is daily count of adult sockeye; the predictor is Qcorr_qPCR (flow-corrected eDNA concentration). Fit, in sequence:

  1. lm(log(Sockeye_Adults+1) ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR+min_pos)) — the old-school approach.
  2. glm(Sockeye_Adults ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR+min_pos) + factor(Year), family=poisson).
  3. glm(Sockeye_Adults ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR+min_pos) + factor(Year), family=quasipoisson).
  4. MASS::glm.nb(Sockeye_Adults ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR+min_pos) + factor(Year)).

For each fit:

  • Examine residual deviance / df. Is overdispersion present?
  • Look at SEs for the eDNA coefficient. Which models give realistically wide vs. unrealistically narrow CIs?
  • Back-transform the log-link slope to a multiplicative effect: "a doubling of eDNA concentration is associated with a ×__ change in expected daily count."
Where the biology lives. If the log-link slope is, say, 0.7, then $e^{0.7 \cdot \ln 2} = 2^{0.7} = 1.62$. So a doubling of eDNA is associated with a 62% increase in sockeye. That sentence is what you put in your paper — not "the slope was 0.7 (SE 0.04)."
Exercise A2 · Titanic logistic regression — long vs aggregated

Titanic survival

Using titanic_long.csv and titanic_prop.csv:

  1. Fit glm(survived ~ class, family=binomial, data=titanic_long).
  2. Fit glm(cbind(Yes, No) ~ Class, family=binomial, data=titanic_prop).
  3. Confirm that coefficients are identical. Why must they be?
  4. Convert each class's log-odds to a probability. Compare to raw proportions.
Reveal solution
library(dplyr)
long <- read.csv("data/titanic_long.csv")
prop <- read.csv("titanic_prop.csv")
f1 <- glm(survived ~ class, family=binomial, data=long)
f2 <- glm(cbind(Yes, No) ~ Class, family=binomial, data=prop)
coef(f1); coef(f2) # same
# Probabilities
plogis(coef(f1)[1] + c(0, coef(f1)[-1]))
prop$Yes / (prop$Yes + prop$No) # raw proportions match

(c) Because the binomial likelihood is the product of Bernoulli likelihoods — aggregating $n$ Bernoullis into one Binomial summary is mathematically equivalent. Only the deviance differs (the Bernoulli form includes a constant that the Binomial form doesn't).

Exercise A3 · Titanic with interactions

Sex × Class effects on survival

Fit glm(survived ~ class * sex + age, family=binomial, data=titanic_long). Use effects::allEffects() to plot fitted probabilities, and use emmeans::emmeans(fit, pairwise ~ class | sex) to compare classes within each sex.

  1. Which pairwise contrast is largest (in odds ratio)?
  2. Why is the simple TukeyHSD on an aov object the wrong way to do this comparison?
  3. Translate the female 1st-vs-3rd-class log-odds difference into a probability difference for a 30-year-old passenger.
Hint and discussion

TukeyHSD assumes a Normal-error linear model; on a logistic GLM we need methods (like emmeans with type="link" or "response") that respect the binomial likelihood and the non-identity link. The ranking and uncertainty differ between scales.

Interaction terms: algebra meets geometry

An interaction in a linear model means "the effect of one variable depends on the value of another." The lab walkthrough (InteractionTerms.R in your originals) demonstrates this with simulated data, 2D heatmaps, and 3D plotly surfaces. Four lessons to leave with:

  1. If the true surface is a plane, there is no interaction. A model with $y \sim x_1 \cdot x_2$ should return an interaction coefficient near zero.
  2. If the surface is twisted (saddle/warped), there is an interaction. The sign of the interaction tells you whether the effect of $x_1$ is amplified or dampened as $x_2$ grows.
  3. Interactions on a probability scale can look very different from interactions on a log-odds scale (compare the binary GLM heatmap with the linear predictor's heatmap).
  4. The phrase "is there an interaction?" depends on the response scale. A model that is additive on the log scale is multiplicative on the raw scale — that's not a bug, it's the link function at work.
A useful sanity check: if you have to log-transform your response, then any "additive" model you fit on the log scale is actually a multiplicative model on the raw scale. So "no interaction" in your fit might mask a real biological interaction, and vice versa. Always be explicit about the scale.
