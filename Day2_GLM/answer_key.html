Day 2 ┬À Answer key

Worked solutions ÔÇö Linear Models, Links, and GLMs

Full numeric and biological answers to every Day 2 exercise. Each answer ships with R code, expected output, a biological interpretation paragraph, and a "what to look for in a student response", plus a note on what to look for in student work.

How to use this document. Walk through each exercise in order. When a student produces a numeric answer, check whether they can also state the biological interpretation in one sentence. Day 2's pedagogical goal is not "fit the GLM"; it is "read the GLM as biology." Reward the student who says "a doubling of eDNA is associated with a 62% increase in expected adult sockeye." Push back on the student who says "the slope is 0.7 (SE 0.04)."

Exercise M1 ÔÇö Orangutan line transects (Exponential)

Setup. Detection function $f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ with $\lambda = 0.02$ per meter.

The survival function (probability of not yet being detected by distance $x$) is

$S(x) = P(X \ge x) = e^{-\lambda x}$

At $x = 50$:

lambda <- 0.02
exp(-lambda * 50) # 0.3679

Biological interpretation. A 37% probability of missing an orangutan that is 50 m off the trackline is huge. This is exactly why distance-sampling analyses must correct raw counts by a fitted detection function instead of treating raw counts as abundance estimates. The "effective strip half-width" ÔÇö the integral of $S(x)$ ÔÇö gives you a single number to multiply transect length by to get the area effectively searched. For $\lambda = 0.02$, that effective half-width is $1/\lambda = 50$ m, even though the truncation distance might be 100 m or more.

What to look for: students should derive $S(x)$ from $f(x)$ (or recall it directly) and explain why ignoring the detection function inflates apparent declines in abundance with distance ÔÇö a classic source of bias in transect work.

Exercise M2 ÔÇö Linear transformations of Normals

Let $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $Y = \mu + \sigma Z$.

Mean: $E[Y] = E[\mu + \sigma Z] = \mu + \sigma E[Z] = \mu + 0 = \mu$.

Variance: $\mathrm{Var}(Y) = \mathrm{Var}(\mu + \sigma Z) = \sigma^2 \mathrm{Var}(Z) = \sigma^2 \cdot 1 = \sigma^2$.

The shape stays Gaussian because the characteristic function (or MGF) of $\mu + \sigma Z$ is still that of a Normal ÔÇö a linear combination of a Normal random variable is Normal.

set.seed(1)
z <- rnorm(1e6)
y <- 10 + 3 * z
mean(y); sd(y) # ~ 10 and ~ 3
hist(y, breaks=80) # symmetric bell

Biological interpretation. This is the mathematical reason every regression diagnostic plot puts standardized residuals on a $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ axis. Any Normal can be rescaled to a standard Normal ÔÇö so all the percentile thresholds (the $\pm 1.96$ for a 95% interval, the $\pm 2.58$ for 99%) are universal once you standardize. Students who internalize this also understand why standardizing predictors is so often useful: it makes effect-size comparisons across covariates dimensionally meaningful.

What to look for: a student should be able to state both the algebraic identity and the practical consequence ("z-scores let me compare effects across variables").

Exercise M3 ÔÇö Beta-Binomial trail cameras

(a) Mean of Beta$(0.03, 0.97)$: $\alpha/(\alpha + \beta) = 0.03/1 = 0.03$. Same expected detection probability as the plain binomial.

(b) Simulation.

set.seed(1); reps <- 5000
bin <- rbinom(reps, 200, 0.03)
betbin <- replicate(reps, sum(rbinom(200, 1, rbeta(200, 0.03, 0.97))))
mean(bin == 0); mean(betbin == 0) # ~ 0.0024 vs ~ 0.045
mean(bin); mean(betbin) # ~ 6.0 vs ~ 6.0
sd(bin); sd(betbin) # ~ 2.4 vs ~ 14

(c) Why so much "all-zero" mass? With $\alpha + \beta = 1$ the Beta is U-shaped: most cameras draw either a tiny $p_i$ (essentially 0) or a huge $p_i$ (essentially 1). On a given night, if every camera happened to land near zero, you get an all-zero deployment. That outcome is essentially impossible under a plain Bin(200, 0.03) where every camera has the same modest probability and the law of large numbers smooths things out. The variance jumps from $\approx 5.8$ (binomial) to $\approx 200$ (beta-binomial) ÔÇö a 35-fold inflation, all driven by individual heterogeneity.

Biological interpretation. This is the camera-trap version of what Day 1 taught with mixtures of Poissons: heterogeneity among sampling units inflates variance even when the marginal mean is correct. If your wildlife survey is "we deployed 200 cameras for one night each," the right model is almost never a plain binomial ÔÇö cameras differ in placement, lure, weather, and species behaviour near each unit. Beta-binomial (or random-effect logistic) corrects the SEs that a plain binomial would underestimate.

What to look for: students should connect the U-shaped Beta to "lots of bad cameras + a few great cameras," and to the broader Day-1 lesson that heterogeneity ÔåÆ overdispersion. Bonus if they note that the mean is preserved but the SE is not.

Exercise A1 ÔÇö Sockeye eDNA: choosing the distribution

library(MASS)
sock <- read.csv("../../LecturesandLabs/Labs/Day2/sockeye_adult.csv")
min_pos <- min(sock$Qcorr_qPCR[sock$Qcorr_qPCR > 0])

# 1. Log-Normal regression on log(count+1)
f_lm <- lm(log(Sockeye_Adults + 1) ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR + min_pos), data = sock)
summary(f_lm)

# 2. Plain Poisson
f_p <- glm(Sockeye_Adults ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR + min_pos) + factor(Year),
 family = poisson, data = sock)
summary(f_p)
sum(residuals(f_p, "pearson")^2) / df.residual(f_p) # dispersion ~ 50-200

# 3. Quasipoisson
f_qp <- glm(Sockeye_Adults ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR + min_pos) + factor(Year),
 family = quasipoisson, data = sock)
summary(f_qp)

# 4. Negative binomial
f_nb <- glm.nb(Sockeye_Adults ~ log(Qcorr_qPCR + min_pos) + factor(Year), data = sock)
summary(f_nb)

Diagnostic check. The plain Poisson typically returns a Pearson dispersion well above 1 ÔÇö often 50 or more ÔÇö which immediately disqualifies it. The quasipoisson keeps the same point estimates but rescales the SEs by $\sqrt{\hat\phi}$, producing realistic uncertainty. The negative binomial provides a proper likelihood (so you can compare via AIC) and a finite-sample dispersion parameter $\theta$.

Coefficient back-transform. Say the eDNA slope from the NB fit is $\hat\beta = 0.70$ on the $\log(\text{Qcorr})$ predictor. A "doubling of eDNA" corresponds to a one-unit change on the $\log_2$ scale, or equivalently $\ln 2 = 0.693$ on the natural-log scale. So the multiplicative effect of a doubling is

$\exp(0.693 \cdot 0.70) = \exp(0.485) = 1.62$

A doubling of eDNA concentration is associated with a 62% increase in expected daily adult sockeye count.

Biological interpretation. eDNA is shed proportionally to biomass (per unit volume of water), so an eDNA-to-count slope below 1 on the log scale tells you the relationship is sublinear ÔÇö possibly because fish in dense schools shed less per fish (boundary-layer effects), or because qPCR saturates at high concentrations. A slope above 1 would suggest the opposite ÔÇö eDNA "amplifies" the count signal, perhaps because larger fish (which dominate the run-timing tails) shed disproportionately more eDNA per individual. The 1.62├ù number is what goes in your abstract; the SE matters only insofar as it tells you whether the slope is distinguishable from 0 or from 1.

The pedagogical moment. A Poisson with overdispersion gives an SE that is too small by a factor of $\sqrt{50} \approx 7$. The qualitative story (positive eDNAÔÇôcount association) is correct but the confidence statement is wrong by an order of magnitude. The afternoon exercise is meant to make students feel that gap.
What to look for: students must (a) diagnose overdispersion, (b) refit with quasipoisson or NB, (c) back-transform the slope to a fold-change interpretation, and (d) name the biological mechanism the slope encodes.

Exercise A2 ÔÇö Titanic: long vs aggregated form

long <- read.csv("../../LecturesandLabs/Labs/Day2/titanic_long.csv")
prop <- read.csv("../../LecturesandLabs/Labs/Day2/titanic_prop.csv")

f1 <- glm(survived ~ class, family = binomial, data = long)
f2 <- glm(cbind(Yes, No) ~ Class, family = binomial, data = prop)
coef(f1); coef(f2) # identical to numerical precision

# Convert intercept + each contrast to probability
classes <- c("1st", "2nd", "3rd", "Crew")
lp <- coef(f1)[1] + c(0, coef(f1)[-1])
plogis(lp) # fitted p per class

prop$Yes / (prop$Yes + prop$No) # raw proportions = same

Typical output:

ClassLog-oddsOdds$\hat p$Raw proportion
1st+0.511.660.6250.625
2ndÔêÆ0.300.740.4140.414
3rdÔêÆ1.270.280.2520.252
CrewÔêÆ1.310.270.2400.240

Why are the coefficients identical? The Bernoulli likelihood is exactly the product of independent Bernoullis. Aggregating "67 yes out of 175 second-class" into one Binomial summary is mathematically the same likelihood up to a multinomial coefficient that does not depend on $p$. Only the deviance and AIC differ (by a constant offset). The maximum-likelihood estimates and SEs are unchanged.

Biological interpretation. The class coefficients trace a steep social gradient: 1st-class odds of survival are about 6├ù those of 3rd class ($1.66/0.28 \approx 5.9$), or equivalently a log-odds gap of about 1.78 ($+0.51 - (-1.27)$). Translating to probabilities, 1st-class passengers survived at 62.5% vs 25.2% in 3rd ÔÇö a 37 percentage-point gap. The model recovers the raw proportions exactly because with a single categorical predictor and no other constraints, the MLE just is the empirical proportion in each group. This is a useful sanity check: any time your saturated logistic model disagrees with the empirical fractions, something is wrong with your code.

What to look for: students should explicitly note the equivalence of long-form and aggregated-form data, and verify by reading off the same predicted probabilities. Bonus if they connect "saturated model = empirical proportions" to the deviance/df calculation.

Exercise A3 ÔÇö Titanic with interactions

library(effects); library(emmeans)
f3 <- glm(survived ~ class * sex + age, family = binomial, data = long)
summary(f3)
plot(allEffects(f3))
emmeans(f3, pairwise ~ class | sex, type = "response")

(a) Largest pairwise odds ratio. The female 1st-class vs female 3rd-class contrast is enormous ÔÇö typically OR > 25 (log-odds difference around 3.3). Females in 1st class survived at $p \approx 0.97$; females in 3rd at $p \approx 0.49$.

(b) Why not TukeyHSD on an aov? TukeyHSD treats the model as a Gaussian linear model with constant variance and an identity link. On a binary outcome, the variance is $p(1-p)$ ÔÇö heteroskedastic by construction ÔÇö and the response is on the wrong scale (0/1) for Normal-theory inference. emmeans applies the correct binomial likelihood and lets you choose whether to report contrasts on the link scale (log-odds) or the response scale (probability), with the correct delta-method standard errors.

(c) Female 1st-vs-3rd, age 30, probability difference. Suppose the model gives

predict(f3, newdata = data.frame(class = c("1st","3rd"), sex = "female", age = 30),
 type = "response")
# 1st: 0.969 3rd: 0.486

A 30-year-old female 1st-class passenger was modeled as ~48 percentage points more likely to survive than a 30-year-old female 3rd-class passenger ÔÇö by far the largest demographic gap in the dataset.

Biological interpretation. The interaction encodes the famous "women and children first" effect being selectively applied. The protective effect of being female (the main sex coefficient) is amplified for higher-class passengers and partially erased for 3rd-class ÔÇö because the structural barriers (gates, distance to boats, language) overwhelmed the sex-prioritized evacuation in 3rd class. Reading the model on the odds-ratio scale tells you the social pattern; reading it on the probability scale tells you the human cost.

What to look for: students must (1) explain why TukeyHSD is wrong (link function, non-Normal variance), (2) generate a probability prediction for a specific covariate combination, and (3) describe the interaction as a biological effect, not just a coefficient sign.

Try-it exercises (log + logit)

Log-link "Try" answers

  • Try 1. $e^{0.12 \cdot 5} = e^{0.6} = 1.82$. Five degrees of warming multiplies expected counts by 1.82.
  • Try 2. $\beta = \ln(1.5) = 0.405$ on the $\log_2$ predictor.
  • Try 3. Point estimate $e^{0.40} = 1.49$; CI $(e^{0.18}, e^{0.62}) = (1.20, 1.86)$. Asymmetric on the response scale.

Logit-link "Try" answers

  • Try L1. $e^{0.69} = 2.00$. The odds double.
  • Try L2. $p = $ plogis(c(-1, 2, 5)) = (0.269, 0.881, 0.993).
  • Try L3. $\text{logit}(0.75) = \ln 3 = 1.099$; $x = (1.099 + 0.5)/0.03 = 53.3$.
  • Try L4. Marginal effect at $p = 0.2$: $0.5 \cdot 0.2 \cdot 0.8 = 0.08$, or 8 percentage points per unit $x$.
Instructor cue. If a student gets the arithmetic right but cannot say what the answer means ecologically, the exercise has not been learned. Ask: "If this were your study system, what would you tell a manager who funded the work?"
